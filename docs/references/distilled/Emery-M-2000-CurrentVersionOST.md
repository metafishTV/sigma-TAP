# Merrelyn Emery (2000) — The Current Version of Emery's Open Systems Theory

**Source**: Merrelyn Emery, "The Current Version of Emery's Open Systems Theory," *Systemic Practice and Action Research*, Vol. 13, No. 5, 2000, pp. 623-643.

**Core contribution**: The most rigorous and complete theoretical statement of OST(E) -- Open Systems Theory as developed by Fred and Merrelyn Emery, distinguished from other systems approaches. Traces the intellectual genealogy through the "thin red line" (Leibniz -> Peirce -> Pepper -> Emery), formalizes the L11/L12/L21/L22 framework with Sommerhoff's directive correlation and Angyal's system principle (unitas multiplex), establishes the fundamental distinction between transaction and interaction, maps the functional level hierarchy (goal-seeking -> purposefulness -> ideal-seeking) to the design principles, and positions OST(E) explicitly within Pepper's contextualism as the only world hypothesis capable of dealing with novelty and change.

---

## 1. The Thin Red Line: Two Streams of Knowledge

The paper opens with the most explicit intellectual genealogy in the OST literature. Emery argues that Western knowledge has flowed through two fundamentally opposed streams, and that the confusion between them has produced most of the conceptual errors in systems thinking:

### Stream 1: Abstract Universals (Idealism)

**Lineage**: Kant -> Newton -> Freud -> Lewin -> most of contemporary systems theory

**Core question**: "What IS it?" (essentialist)

**Method**: Analysis -- decomposing wholes into parts, seeking the essence within the entity

**Verification**: Multiplicative corroboration -- replication of findings across identical conditions. The same experiment repeated must produce the same result.

**Unit of analysis**: The entity itself. Sufficient conditions for behavior are sought WITHIN the organism/system. Self-action (the entity acts from its own nature) or inter-action (entities exchange without mutual change).

**World hypothesis**: Mechanism, organicism, or formism (Pepper's classification). All three are closed and static -- they deal with entities that have fixed natures and interact without genuine novelty.

### Stream 2: Material Universals (Realism -- The Thin Red Line)

**Lineage**: Leibniz -> Peirce -> Pepper -> Fred Emery -> OST(E)

**Core question**: "What does it DO?" (functional)

**Method**: Synthesis -- understanding parts through their relation to the whole and to the environment. The emphasis is on putting together, not taking apart.

**Verification**: Structural corroboration -- invariants found across different contexts. The SAME structural pattern found in coal mining, education, manufacturing, and community development corroborates the design principle theory not because the contexts are identical but because the structural invariant (DP1 or DP2) is found despite contextual variation.

**Unit of analysis**: The system-in-environment. Sufficient conditions for behavior are sought in the RELATIONSHIP between system and environment, not within the system alone. Transaction -- mutual influence where both parties are changed by the encounter.

**World hypothesis**: Contextualism (Pepper). The only world hypothesis that deals with novelty and change. Events are unique, contextual, emergent. Laws are not universal abstractions but structural invariants found through structural corroboration across diverse contexts.

### Why the Distinction Matters

The thin red line argument is not merely historical. It explains why most systems theory went wrong:

- **General Systems Theory (GST)**: Began with promise (Bertalanffy's open systems concept) but spawned variations on mechanism, organicism, and formism rather than developing contextualism. GST became "part of the problem" because it applied closed-system thinking (analysis, abstract universals, self-action/interaction) to open systems that require transactional, contextual analysis.

- **Lewin**: Despite his field theory innovations, remained within the idealist stream. Lewinian action research uses the group as its unit (not system-in-environment) and seeks causal mechanisms within group dynamics rather than in the system-environment transaction.

- **Freud**: Located the sufficient conditions of behavior entirely within the individual psyche. The environment appears only as stimulus or frustration, not as a co-constitutive partner in an ongoing transaction.

Fred Emery's contribution was to trace the thin red line through Peirce and Pepper and build a systems theory on genuinely transactional, contextual foundations -- something no other systems theorist had accomplished.

## 2. L11/L12/L21/L22: The Full Formal Treatment

The paper provides the most rigorous formal treatment of the L-matrix in the OST literature:

### L11: The System Principle

L11 captures the system's internal organization -- not merely its parts but the PRINCIPLE by which parts relate to each other and to the whole. This is Angyal's unitas multiplex: the system is a unity of multiplicities. System members relate to each other THROUGH their relation to the whole, not through pairwise connections.

L11 is not reducible to the sum of its parts or to the network of pairwise relationships between parts. It is the organizing principle -- the structural invariant that makes the collection of parts a system rather than an aggregate. Under DP1, the L11 principle is hierarchical coordination (parts relate through their position in the chain of command). Under DP2, the L11 principle is shared responsibility for a whole task (parts relate through their contribution to the shared goal).

### L12: The Planning Function

L12 captures the system's action on its environment -- the system plans, acts, and projects into its environment. This is the system's agency: its capacity to change the conditions of its own existence by modifying the environment.

L12 is the planning function in the full sense: not just formulating plans but executing them -- producing outputs, delivering services, creating products, building relationships, generating effects in the world beyond the system boundary.

### L21: The Learning Function

L21 captures the environment's action on the system -- the system receives, absorbs, and adapts to environmental inputs. This is the system's receptivity: its capacity to be changed by its environment.

L21 is the learning function: the system acquires information, resources, challenges, and constraints from the environment and incorporates them into its internal organization (L11). Learning is not passive reception but active incorporation -- the system selects, filters, interprets, and integrates environmental signals according to its existing L11 principle.

### L22: The Extended Social Field of Directive Correlations

L22 captures the environment's own internal dynamics -- the causal texture of the environment, which operates under laws that may be incommensurate with the system's internal laws (L11).

L22 is described as "the extended social field of directive correlations" -- the environment is not a passive backdrop but a field of active, directed processes. These processes create the conditions to which the system must adapt but which the system does not control and may not even perceive.

### Coimplication of L11 and L22

The paper makes a strong coimplication claim: L11 and L22 are not independent but mutually determining. The system's internal organization (L11) is shaped by the environmental causal texture (L22), AND the environmental causal texture is shaped by the system's actions (L12) and their downstream effects. Neither can be understood in isolation.

This coimplication is not circular causation (A causes B causes A) but structural mutual determination: the system IS what it is because of its environment, AND the environment IS what it is because of the systems embedded in it. They are aspects of a single system-in-environment, not separable entities that happen to interact.

## 3. Directive Correlation (Sommerhoff)

The paper introduces Sommerhoff's concept of directive correlation as the formal mechanism underlying adaptive behavior:

### Definition

Directive correlation is the necessary condition for an adaptive relationship between system and environment. It requires that:

1. System and environment are at **exact correspondence** for a goal -- their states are correlated in terms of direction at a given time
2. This correlation holds across time: from t0 (initial conditions) through t1 (intermediate states) to t2 (goal state)
3. The correlation is with respect to a specific goal or set of goals -- it is not random correspondence but directed

### Scope

Directive correlation is broader than purposive behavior. It encompasses:

- **Purposive behavior**: The system deliberately acts to maintain or achieve goal correspondence with the environment. This is the most obvious form.
- **Coevolution**: System and environment change together in correlated ways without either "intending" the correlation. The correlation emerges from the structural coupling between them.
- **Potential directive correlation**: The capacity to IMAGINE and EXPECT future environmental states and to prepare for them. This is the source of human ideals -- the ability to conceive of states that do not yet exist but could be brought into existence through directed action.

### Potential Directive Correlation and Ideals

The most significant extension: potential directive correlation = the capacity to envision and work toward states that have never existed. This is not mere prediction (extrapolating from current trends) but genuine ideal-seeking -- conceiving of a desirable state and acting to bring it about.

Fred Emery argued that this capacity for potential directive correlation is what distinguishes purposeful from merely purposive systems. Purposive systems adapt to existing environmental states. Purposeful systems create new environmental states by imagining and enacting ideals.

## 4. Transaction vs Interaction: The Fundamental Distinction

The paper establishes this as THE foundational ontological distinction for OST(E):

### Transaction

- **Definition**: Mutual influence where BOTH parties are changed by the encounter. The transaction produces something new that neither party contained before.
- **Character**: Synthetic -- puts together rather than takes apart. Creates novelty. Open-system process.
- **Appropriate for**: People, organizations, social systems, living systems. Any system that is genuinely open and capable of change through engagement with its environment.

### Interaction

- **Definition**: Exchange without mutual change. Parties exchange inputs/outputs but remain structurally unchanged by the exchange. What goes in comes out (perhaps in different form) but neither party is transformed.
- **Character**: Analytic -- takes apart rather than puts together. Preserves existing states. Closed-system process.
- **Appropriate for**: Machines, mechanical systems, formally defined exchange relationships. Systems whose internal structure is not affected by their exchanges.

### Fred Emery's Formulation

The paper quotes Fred Emery's definitive statement: "With self action and interactionism the emphasis is on analysis: with transactionalism the emphasis is on synthesis."

This maps the transaction/interaction distinction onto the two streams of knowledge: interaction belongs to the idealist stream (analysis, abstract universals, closed systems), transaction belongs to the realist stream (synthesis, material universals, open systems).

### Why This Matters for Organizational Theory

Most organizational theory treats people as if they INTERACT -- they exchange labor for wages, information for information, services for payment. This is the closed-system assumption dressed in organizational language. OST(E) insists that people TRANSACT -- every encounter between people potentially changes both parties. A meeting is not an exchange of information but a transaction that changes the participants' understanding, relationships, and capacity for future action.

The design principles operationalize this distinction: DP1 treats people as interacting parts (exchangeable, unchanged by their exchanges, analyzed separately). DP2 treats people as transacting members (mutually influencing, changed by encounters, understood through their relations to each other and the whole).

## 5. Angyal's System Principle: Unitas Multiplex

The paper presents Angyal's contribution as foundational to OST(E)'s understanding of L11:

### The System Principle

The system principle (unitas multiplex -- unity of multiplicities) asserts that system members relate to each other THROUGH their relation to the whole. This is fundamentally different from network models where members relate through pairwise connections.

In a network, the connection between A and B is a direct relationship between A and B. In a system (as Angyal defines it), the connection between A and B exists because both A and B are related to the system as a whole. Remove the system principle and the connection between A and B dissolves -- not because they are disconnected but because the basis of their connection (the whole) has been removed.

### Autonomy and Homonomy

Angyal identifies two fundamental tendencies in systems:

- **Autonomy**: The tendency toward self-governance, expansion, differentiation. The system develops its own internal complexity and extends its range.
- **Homonomy**: The tendency toward interdependence, integration, belonging. The system participates in larger wholes and finds its function in relation to its environment.

These are not opposed but complementary. A healthy system is BOTH autonomous (self-governing) AND homonomous (integrated with its environment). The pathological alternatives are:
- Autonomy without homonomy = isolation, closed system, death
- Homonomy without autonomy = absorption, loss of identity, dissolution

### Life Between Organism and Environment

Angyal's radical claim, adopted by OST(E): life takes place BETWEEN organism and environment, not inside either one. The system-in-environment IS the living unit. Seeking the causes of behavior inside the organism (Freud) or inside the environment (behaviorism) is equally mistaken -- behavior is a property of the system-environment transaction.

## 6. Pepper's Contextualism: The World Hypothesis

The paper explicitly positions OST(E) within Pepper's classification of world hypotheses:

### The Four World Hypotheses

**Formism**: The world is composed of types, forms, essences. Knowledge proceeds by classification -- identifying what type a thing belongs to. Static, closed.

**Mechanism**: The world is a machine. Knowledge proceeds by identifying causal chains -- input -> process -> output. Static, closed. Reductionist.

**Organicism**: The world is an organism. Knowledge proceeds by identifying wholes and their developmental stages. Deals with development but assumes a telos -- the organism has a determined endpoint. Partially open but ultimately closed (the endpoint is given).

**Contextualism**: The world is composed of events in context. Knowledge proceeds by understanding the unique configuration of factors that produced a specific event. Genuinely open -- deals with novelty, change, emergence, and the irreducibly unique character of every event.

### OST(E) as Contextualist

OST(E) is explicitly and exclusively contextualist. This is not a preference but a structural commitment:

- **Novelty is real**: New types, new configurations, new relationships genuinely emerge. They are not rearrangements of pre-existing elements (mechanism) or stages of a predetermined development (organicism) but novel events in unique contexts.
- **Change is fundamental**: The world is not a static structure that sometimes changes but a continuous process of change that sometimes produces temporary stabilities.
- **Context is constitutive**: The meaning, function, and nature of any element depends on its context. Remove an element from its context and it becomes something different.

### GST's Failure

GST (General Systems Theory) failed because it tried to be "general" -- applying to all systems regardless of context. This generality pulled it toward mechanism (general laws applicable everywhere) and away from contextualism (unique events in unique contexts). The result: GST spawned variations on formism, mechanism, and organicism (system archetypes, system dynamics, viable systems model) but never developed a genuinely contextual systems theory. OST(E) is the correction -- a systems theory that takes context, novelty, and change as primary.

## 7. Environmental Types: Refined Treatment

The paper refines the four environmental types from the 1965 Emery-Trist paper:

### Type II: Cooperative (Stable Values)

The paper provides a historical claim: Type II environments (placid, clustered, with stable values enabling strategic action) characterized Western societies from early agricultural settlement through approximately 1790. Communities had stable value systems, cooperative social structures, and environments that were complex but not dynamic. The DP2 structure was natural to these communities -- self-managing groups organized around whole tasks (farming, crafting, community governance).

### Type III: Competitive (Industrial Revolution)

The Industrial Revolution (approximately 1790 onward) created Type III environments: multiple organizations of the same kind competing in shared markets. This imposed DP1 on populations that had previously lived under DP2:

- **Factory system** fragmented whole tasks into subtasks, assigned one subtask per worker, placed supervisors over workers
- **DP1 was IMPOSED**: It was not a natural evolution but a structural imposition driven by the factory owners' need for control over the production process
- **Group life was SUPPRESSED**: The self-managing work groups of pre-industrial life were deliberately destroyed because they resisted DP1 discipline

The paper is explicit: DP1 did not emerge naturally from increasing complexity. It was imposed by those who controlled capital and production on those who performed labor. This is a political claim embedded in the theoretical framework.

### Type IV: Turbulent (Post-1945)

Type IV environments emerged between 1945 and 1953. The paper specifies this narrow window, suggesting that the combination of postwar reconstruction, nuclear weapons, decolonization, and global interconnection created a qualitatively new environmental condition.

Type IV is characterized by dynamic systems embedded in a dynamic field -- both the organizations and the ground on which they operate are in motion. Two assumptions that held under Types I-III are discarded:

1. **The environment is passive**: Under Type IV, the environment has its own dynamics (L22) that produce change independently of organizational action
2. **Organizations can adapt independently**: Under Type IV, no single organization can adapt on its own -- the environmental dynamics exceed individual adaptive capacity

### The Goal: Modern Type II

The paper's normative claim: the goal of active adaptation is not to manage Type IV turbulence indefinitely but to transform the social field toward a modern form of Type II: "associative, joyful, and wise." This modern Type II retains the cooperative values and stable social structure of pre-industrial Type II but at a higher level of complexity -- incorporating modern technology, global interconnection, and democratic governance.

This is ideal-seeking: not returning to a past state but creating a genuinely new state that embodies the structural virtues (cooperation, stability, DP2) of the old at the complexity level of the new.

## 8. Four Ideals Corresponding to Four L-Parameters

The paper identifies four ideals that guide active adaptation. While the paper does not provide an explicit mapping to L-channels, the structural correspondence is implied:

1. **Homonomy**: The ideal of belonging, integration, finding one's place in larger wholes. Corresponds structurally to L21 (environment -> system: the system finds its function through what it receives from and contributes to the environment).

2. **Nurturance**: The ideal of cultivating means, supporting growth, providing resources. Corresponds structurally to L12 (system -> environment: the system acts on the environment to cultivate conditions for flourishing).

3. **Humanity**: The ideal of what is fitting for us as human beings. Corresponds structurally to L11 (system principle: the internal organization reflects human values and human nature).

4. **Beauty**: The ideal of aesthetic order, harmony, rightness of form. Corresponds structurally to L22 (environmental causal texture: the ideal of an environment whose own dynamics are harmonious and supportive).

These four ideals are not preferences but structural attractors: they represent the states toward which an actively adaptive system-in-environment moves when it is capable of ideal-seeking (potential directive correlation).

## 9. Functional Level Hierarchy

The paper establishes a three-level hierarchy of system functioning:

### Level 1: Goal-Seeking

The simplest adaptive level. The system pursues specific, predetermined goals. The goals are given (by external authority, by programming, by instinct); the system merely seeks them.

**Organizational correlate**: Routine operations, standardized processes, compliance with directives. The organization does what it is told.

### Level 2: Purposefulness

A higher adaptive level. The system can choose AMONG goals and select the most appropriate for current conditions. The system has purpose -- it can evaluate alternative goals and select those that best serve its interests.

**Organizational correlate**: Strategic planning, market positioning, competitive analysis. The organization decides what to do.

**DP1's limit**: DP1 organizations can achieve purposefulness at the organizational level (management chooses goals) but restrict most members to goal-seeking (workers pursue management's goals). The workers are instruments of the organization's purpose, not purposeful agents in their own right.

### Level 3: Ideal-Seeking

The highest adaptive level. The system can conceive of and pursue ideals -- states that have never existed but could be brought into being through directed action. This requires potential directive correlation: the capacity to imagine, expect, and act toward unprecedented states.

**Organizational correlate**: Innovation, transformation, social contribution, value creation. The organization creates what has not existed.

**DP2's enabling function**: Only DP2 allows ideal-seeking at all levels. Under DP2, members are not instruments of the organization but purposeful agents using the organization as an instrument for their own ideal-seeking. This gives DP2 organizations "one more degree of freedom" than DP1 organizations -- the freedom of ALL members to contribute their ideal-seeking capacity, not just management.

## 10. Active Adaptation: The Two-Stage Model in Theoretical Context

The paper presents the two-stage model (SC + PDW) in its full theoretical context:

### Stage 1: Search Conference -- Changing the L11 <-> L22 Relation

The SC changes how the system relates to its environment. Through environmental scanning (L22), econiche analysis (L12/L21), and system history (L11), participants develop a new understanding of their system-in-environment that enables ideal-seeking. The SC's output is not merely strategic direction but a transformed relationship between the system and its environment -- participants SEE the environment differently and SEE their system differently after the SC.

### Stage 2: PDW -- Changing Intra-L11

The PDW changes the system's internal organization. Through analysis of the current DP1 structure, education in design principles, and self-designed restructuring to DP2, workers transform the L11 principle from hierarchical coordination to shared responsibility.

### The Sequence

The two stages must proceed in this order: first change the system-environment relationship (SC), then change the internal structure (PDW). This sequence ensures that the new internal structure (DP2) is aligned with the environmental demands identified in the SC. Reversing the sequence -- restructuring before environmental analysis -- risks producing an internally coherent but environmentally maladapted organization.

### Puzzle Learning vs Problem Solving

The paper distinguishes two modes of learning:

- **Problem solving**: The endpoint is KNOWN. The task is to find a path from the current state to the known endpoint. This is Type II/III adaptation -- the environment is understood well enough to specify goals.

- **Puzzle learning**: The endpoint is UNKNOWN. The task is to discover both the path AND the destination through exploration. This is Type IV adaptation -- the environment is too dynamic and complex to specify goals in advance. The goal emerges through the process of engaging with the environment.

The SC is a puzzle learning process -- participants do not know in advance what strategic direction they will identify. The direction emerges from the process of environmental scanning, system analysis, and integration.

### Strategy of the Indirect Approach

The paper references Sun Tzu's strategy of the indirect approach: active adaptation does not attack problems head-on but approaches them obliquely. The SC does not begin with "what is our problem?" but with "what is happening in our environment?" -- the indirect approach that reveals the problem's true nature and the space of possible responses.

### Embryos of Social Change

The SC is designed to produce "embryos of social change" -- early, small-scale manifestations of the desired future that can grow and propagate. These embryos are not pilot projects (management-designed, controlled, evaluated) but organic growths (participant-initiated, self-organizing, self-propagating). The SC creates the conditions for embryos to emerge; it does not design them.

### Rationalization of Conflict vs Consensus

The paper distinguishes the SC's approach from consensus-based methods:

- **Consensus** (Trist, Ackoff): Seeks agreement. Differences are resolved through discussion until all parties agree. This can suppress genuine disagreement and produce false unity.

- **Rationalization of conflict** (Emerys): Does NOT seek agreement. Conflicts are surfaced, their structural sources identified, and their implications analyzed. Some conflicts are resolved through shared understanding; others persist as genuine, irresolvable differences that the community must learn to manage. The goal is not unity but clarity -- understanding WHY people disagree and WHAT structural conditions produce the disagreement.

## 11. The Learning Organization

The paper provides a precise definition:

"A learning organization is an organization structured in such a way that its members can learn and continue to learn within it."

Key clarifications:

- **Organizations cannot learn**: They have no nervous systems, no consciousness, no capacity for experience. The phrase "organizational learning" is a category error. PEOPLE learn; organizations provide (or prevent) the conditions for learning.

- **DP1 prevents learning**: Under DP1, most members are restricted to narrow, routinized tasks that provide no learning opportunity. Even when training is provided, the DP1 structure prevents practice of new skills. The organization may invest in training programs while its structure makes that investment worthless.

- **Only DP2 produces a learning organization**: Under DP2, the structural conditions for learning (adequate autonomy, ongoing learning opportunity, variety, mutual support, meaningfulness, desirable future) are embedded in the design. Learning is not a program but a property of the structure.

## 12. Post-Tavistock Divergences

The paper documents a critical intellectual split within the socio-technical tradition:

### The Emery Path

- **Rationalization of conflict** (not consensus) as the basis for integration
- **Time-based Search Conference** (environmentally grounded, historically aware, future-oriented)
- **Genotypical design principles** (DP1/DP2) as the lever for structural change
- **Participative Design Workshop** as the implementation method
- **Workers design their own structure** (not consultants, not management, not committees)

### The Trist/Ackoff Path

- **Consensus** as the basis for integration
- **Time-free idealized design** (imagining the ideal without historical or environmental constraint)
- **Domain theory** (sectors/domains rather than design principles)
- **Committees** as the governance mechanism (= DP1 by another name)

### The Crucial Divergence

The paper is emphatic: neither Trist nor Ackoff ever used the genotypical design principles or the PDW. This is not a minor methodological difference but a fundamental theoretical split:

- Trist's domain theory and Ackoff's idealized design do not address the coordination structure (DP1 vs DP2). They can produce elaborate strategic visions while leaving the DP1 hierarchy intact.
- Committees are DP1 structures: they locate responsibility with representatives (one level above the people affected), not with the people themselves. Using committees to implement participative design is a structural contradiction.
- Consensus can suppress genuine disagreement and produce apparent agreement that lacks structural foundation. Rationalization of conflict produces understanding that may include persistent disagreement but is structurally honest.

The Emery path is the only path that changes the genotype (the design principle). All other approaches, including Trist's and Ackoff's, are phenotypical -- they change surface features while leaving DP1 in place.

## Key Concepts for sigma-TAP Integration

### 1. Transaction vs Interaction = The Fundamental Agent Architecture Distinction

This is THE foundational distinction for sigma-TAP agent architecture. Agents TRANSACT -- they mutually influence and change each other. They do NOT merely interact -- exchanging information or resources without mutual change. Every cross-metathesis event must change BOTH participants, not just transfer information from one to the other.

This is already partially captured by absorptive cross-metathesis (one agent absorbs the other's types, the other becomes dormant). But the Emery framework demands MORE: both agents should be changed by every encounter, not just the receiver. Even the "giving" agent in an absorptive cross-metathesis is changed by the giving -- its L12 count increments, its TAPS signature shifts, its relationship to the receiving agent is altered. The L-matrix already tracks this, but the EXPERIENTIAL change (the giving agent becoming different through having given) needs to be reflected in the agent's state, not just its counters.

For novel cross-metathesis: both parents become dormant while the child spawns. This IS mutual change -- both parents are transformed (from active to dormant) by the encounter. The current model captures this structurally.

### 2. Directive Correlation = The Formal Mechanism Underlying sigma(Xi)

Sommerhoff's directive correlation provides the formal definition of what sigma(Xi) measures: the degree of correspondence between the agent's trajectory and the environmental field, correlated in terms of direction. When sigma is high, the agent is in directive correlation with its environment -- its actions (L12) are correlated with environmental states (L22) in a way that produces adaptive outcomes. When sigma approaches 0, the agent is maladapted -- no directive correlation with the environment, actions produce no adaptive value.

The time dimension is critical: directive correlation is not a snapshot but a trajectory (t0 -> t1 -> t2). sigma(Xi) should capture not just current correspondence but the DIRECTION of correspondence -- whether the agent is becoming more or less adapted over time. This is the difference between purposive adaptation (tracking current environmental state) and ideal-seeking (tracking potential future environmental states that do not yet exist).

Potential directive correlation (the capacity to imagine and expect) maps to the TAPS signature's T-component (how I become) -- the agent's capacity to envision and pursue states that are not yet realized.

### 3. Angyal's Unitas Multiplex = The System Principle Governing L11

Each agent's system principle is its TAPS signature -- the unique pattern of how it becomes (T), how it is (A), how it acts (P), how it creates (S). Members of a group relate to each other THROUGH the system principle (the shared commitment to a whole task, the shared TAPS orientation), not through pairwise connections.

This validates the user's field architecture over pairwise interaction models. The L-matrix records pairwise events (L12, L21), but the SYSTEM PRINCIPLE (L11) is not a pairwise relationship -- it is the agent's relation to the whole. In sigma-TAP: the group's L11 is not the sum of pairwise L12/L21 exchanges but the emergent principle that organizes those exchanges. Two groups with identical pairwise exchange patterns but different L11 principles (different TAPS signatures, different design principles, different values) will behave differently because the organizing principle is different.

Autonomy and homonomy map to two tendencies in every agent: the tendency toward self-development (L11 self-metathesis, type_set expansion, individual trajectory) and the tendency toward integration (L21 absorption, group membership, shared goals). Both are necessary; either alone is pathological (autonomy without homonomy = isolation/death; homonomy without autonomy = absorption/dissolution).

### 4. Four Ideals Map to Four L-Channels (and Potentially to TAPS)

The four ideals (Homonomy, Nurturance, Humanity, Beauty) map to the four L-channels:

| Ideal | L-Channel | TAPS Letter (Speculative) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Homonomy | L21 | A (how I am) | Belonging, integration, being-in-relation |
| Nurturance | L12 | P (how I act) | Cultivating, supporting, producing effects |
| Humanity | L11 | T (how I become) | What is fitting, the system principle, identity |
| Beauty | L22 | S (how I create) | Aesthetic order, environmental harmony, creation |

This mapping is speculative but structurally promising. If validated, it would provide a deep theoretical grounding for the TAPS signature: each TAPS letter would correspond not only to an L-channel and a temporal mode but to a fundamental human ideal. The TAPS signature would then be readable as a profile of ideal-orientation: an agent with dominant T is oriented toward humanity (what is fitting for us), while an agent with dominant S is oriented toward beauty (aesthetic order in the environment).

### 5. Contextualism = The World Hypothesis for sigma-TAP

OST(E) is explicitly contextual, not mechanistic. sigma-TAP must model novelty and change (contextualism), not equilibrium and stability (mechanism). The TAP equation already does this: the adjacent possible is a contextual concept -- what is adjacent depends on what exists NOW, in THIS configuration, in THIS context. There is no universal adjacent possible; it is always local, temporal, and contextual.

But the organizational layer must also be contextual: topology must be emergent (arising from specific interaction histories in specific contexts), not imposed (derived from universal structural laws). Design principles (DP1/DP2) are structural INVARIANTS found through structural corroboration (they appear in coal mines, factories, schools, communities, and simulations), but they are not abstract universals -- they are concrete patterns that emerge in specific contexts and must be discovered anew in each context.

For sigma-TAP verification: structural corroboration, not multiplicative corroboration. The test of the model is not "does it reproduce the same results under identical conditions?" (replication) but "does it produce the same structural invariants (DP1/DP2 attractors, Type I-IV environmental dynamics, six criteria effects) under DIFFERENT conditions?" The same genotype appearing across different parameter settings, initial conditions, and simulation configurations is the strongest validation.

### 6. Environmental Type Regression/Progression

The goal of active adaptation = modern Type II (cooperative, stable values, DP2 structure). In sigma-TAP: the endpoint of successful simulation should show groups converging toward cooperative, stable-valued configurations (DP2 attractors with shared ideals) rather than competitive fragmentation (Type III/DP1) or chaotic turbulence (Type IV). But this convergence is not guaranteed -- environmental dynamics (L22) can push toward Type III or Type IV, and organizational failure can produce regression from Type II toward Type I (randomized, isolated agents).

The environmental type is itself an emergent property of the simulation, not a parameter. A simulation that produces stable DP2 groups in a cooperative social field has produced Type II conditions. A simulation that produces DP1 hierarchies competing for resources has produced Type III. A simulation where the environment's own dynamics overwhelm all organizational responses has produced Type IV. The environmental type should be MEASURED, not set.

### 7. Two-Stage Model = Two Phases of sigma-TAP Architecture

The SC -> PDW sequence maps to the user's planned architecture for Stage 3C+:

- **Phase 1 (sigma feedback + trust = SC)**: Changes the L11 <-> L22 relation. Agents develop environmental awareness (sigma feedback tells them how well they are adapted), accumulate trust metrics (tau records how reliably other agents cooperate), and begin to form ideal-seeking orientations (potential directive correlation). This is the "environmental scanning" phase of the architecture.

- **Phase 2 (topology/family groups = PDW)**: Changes intra-L11. Agents restructure their coordination relationships based on environmental awareness and trust accumulated in Phase 1. Self-managing groups emerge (DP2 attractors), family lineages develop, and the organizational topology stabilizes around structural invariants. This is the "organizational redesign" phase.

### 8. Embryos of Social Change = Early Metathetic Events as Paradigm Shift Indicators

The SC's "embryos of social change" concept maps to early metathetic events that may indicate value shifts. In sigma-TAP: detecting when a small number of agents begin novel cross-metathesis patterns that deviate from the group norm could be an early indicator of paradigm shift. These deviations are not noise -- they are embryos of a new organizational form. If the embryos grow (more agents adopt the new pattern), a paradigm shift may be underway. If they die (the deviating agents are absorbed back into the norm or disintegrate), the current paradigm is stable.

The key diagnostic question: are novel cross-metathesis events clustered in a few agents (embryos forming) or distributed randomly across the population (noise)? Clustered novelty = emergent paradigm shift. Distributed novelty = background innovation within the current paradigm.

### 9. Puzzle Learning vs Problem Solving = Two Innovation Modes

In sigma-TAP: absorptive cross-metathesis is problem solving (the endpoint -- a known type_set configuration -- is given; the agent seeks it by absorbing existing types). Novel cross-metathesis is puzzle learning (the endpoint -- a new type that has never existed -- is unknown; it emerges from the combination of parent types). The simulation should produce both modes, with their relative frequency depending on environmental conditions: stable environments favor problem solving (absorptive), turbulent environments demand puzzle learning (novel).

### 10. Learning Organization = DP2 as Structural Prerequisite

Only DP2 produces a learning organization. In sigma-TAP: groups under DP2 conditions (self-managing coordination, shared responsibility, redundancy of functions) should show continuous type_set growth, rising TAPS signature diversity, and temporal state progression. Groups under DP1 conditions should show type_set atrophy, signature homogenization, and temporal state stagnation or regression. This is a directly testable prediction: measure type_set diversity and temporal state trajectories under DP1 vs DP2 conditions and verify that DP2 produces learning (growth, diversification, progression) while DP1 prevents it (atrophy, homogenization, stagnation).
