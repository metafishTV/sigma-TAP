## Core Argument

DeLanda reconstructs Deleuze and Guattari's assemblage theory as a rigorous ontological framework by performing three operations simultaneously: clarification (stripping away D&G's deliberately obscure prose), systematization (organizing scattered concepts into a coherent parametrized framework), and empirical grounding (demonstrating the framework through concrete case studies in economics, linguistics, military history, chemistry, and mathematics) [Introduction, pp. 1-10]. The central move is replacing D&G's sometimes binary distinction between strata and assemblages with a *continuously variable parametrized concept* — territorialization and coding become parameters that can be set to different values, placing any given whole somewhere along a continuum rather than in a fixed category [Introduction, pp. 3-5]. This parametrization is the book's key methodological innovation: it transforms assemblage theory from a vocabulary of binary oppositions into a framework of variable settings applicable to any scale of organization.

The argument unfolds through three structural layers. The first layer (Chapters 1-4) demonstrates assemblage theory's empirical reach by applying it to nested social and natural entities: persons, interpersonal networks, institutional organizations, cities, and nation-states are each analyzed as assemblages with characteristic settings of territorialization and coding parameters [Ch. 1, pp. 10-34]; languages are treated as assemblages whose material (phonological/lexical) and expressive (semantic/pragmatic) components interact through variable coding [Ch. 2, pp. 35-45]; weapons and military assemblages demonstrate the properties/capacities distinction and double articulation [Ch. 3, pp. 46-56]; scientific fields are reconceived as populations of practitioners, instruments, and phenomena rather than monolithic paradigms [Ch. 4, pp. 57-70].

The second layer (Chapters 5-6) develops the ontological infrastructure: possibility spaces structured by singularities (attractors, bifurcations) constitute the *virtual* dimension of assemblages — what is real but not actual [Ch. 5, pp. 71-88]. Actual assemblages are *solutions* to problems defined by virtual structure, and the relationship between virtual singularities and actual entities is one of *divergent actualization*: the same topological distribution of singularities can be actualized in qualitatively different phenomena without resemblance between virtual cause and actual effect [Ch. 6, pp. 89-105]. This replaces essentialist ontology (natural kinds with fixed essences) with a realist ontology of individuation processes driven by symmetry-breaking cascades.

The third layer (Chapter 7) synthesizes by arguing that assemblages are solutions to objective problems, where problems are defined by the virtual structure of possibility spaces and *survive* their solutions — the problem of flow persists whether a fluid adopts laminar, convective, or turbulent movement [Ch. 7, pp. 106-120]. The book culminates in extending this framework from continuous possibility spaces (state space, attractors) to discrete possibility spaces (cellular automata, rule spaces), arguing that even capacities — not just tendencies — have virtual structure awaiting philosophical investigation [Ch. 7, pp. 117-120].

| Assemblage (agencement) | Wholes whose properties emerge from interactions between heterogeneous parts, where parts maintain autonomy via relations of exteriority | The master concept: replaces both organic totalities (parts defined by whole) and aggregates (no emergent properties). Emergence WITHOUT loss of component autonomy — components can be detached and plugged into different assemblages | Introduction, pp. 1-3 |

Replaces D&G's filiation (interiority) vs alliance (exteriority) distinction | Introduction, pp. 1-2 |
| Relations of interiority | Parts defined constitutively by their relations — removing a part destroys both part and whole | The organism model that assemblage theory replaces. Hegel's seamless totalities, where parts are logically necessary to the whole. DeLanda argues most real wholes are NOT like this | Introduction, p. 2 |
| Territorialization / Deterritorialization | Variable parameter measuring degree of spatial/boundary rigidity. High = sharp boundaries, fixed territory, internal homogeneity. Organizations from rigid bureaucracies (high T) to flexible networks (low T). The same entity can shift along this parameter over time | Introduction, pp. 3-5; Ch. 1, pp. 12-19 |
| Coding / Decoding | Variable parameter measuring degree of genetic/normative determination. High = rigid rules, strict genetic programs, fixed ritual. Low = flexible learning, variable behavior, adaptive responses | The second axis of the parametrized assemblage concept. Coding governs the *expressive* role: how an assemblage signals its identity and membership. Decoding = substituting flexible, adaptive responses for rigid genetic/normative programs | Introduction, pp. 3-5; Ch. 1, pp. 19-23 |
| Double articulation | Two-step process: first articulation selects/sorts material from a wider substrate (pebbles by size); second articulation cements/consolidates sorted material into a new entity (sedimentary rock) | Explains how assemblages at one scale become components at the next scale. First articulation produces statistical order (frequency distributions); second produces durable structure. Maps to D&G's content/expression distinction, but DeLanda prefers material/expressive | Ch. 1, pp. 19-22 |
| Properties vs Capacities | Properties: actual, enduring states measurable without interaction (length, weight, color). Capacities: virtual, exercised only in events of interaction, potentially infinite list | A knife's sharpness is a property; its capacity to cut wood differs from its capacity to cut rope — capacities depend on what the entity *interacts with*. Properties are actual/extensive; capacities are virtual/intensive. This grounds the actual/virtual distinction empirically | Ch. 3, pp. 49-51 |
| Intensive vs Extensive properties | Extensive: additive, divisible without qualitative change (length, volume, weight). Intensive: subject to critical thresholds producing qualitative phase transitions (temperature, speed, pressure, density) | Intensive differences DRIVE individuation — they are the engine of production. Extensive properties result from individuation processes. A population of intensive differences underlies every assemblage; cancelling intensive differences = reaching thermodynamic death | Ch. 5, pp. 72-75 |
| Virtual / Actual | Virtual: real but not actual — the topological structure of possibility spaces (singularities, attractors, bifurcations). Actual: manifest, determined, metric. NOT the possible/real distinction | The virtual is fully real — it has causal efficacy (tendencies, capacities). But it exists in a different mode than actual entities. Key: the virtual does NOT resemble its actualizations (divergent actualization). A topological singularity can be actualized as a soap bubble, a crystal, or a light ray | Ch. 5, pp. 71-73; Ch. 6, pp. 89-96 |
| State space + Control space | State space: structured by singularities (point attractors = steady states, limit cycles = oscillations, strange attractors = chaos). Control space: structured by bifurcations (transitions between attractor configurations) | Together constitute the assemblage's *diagram* — its virtual component. State space shows what an assemblage tends toward; control space shows how it transitions between regimes. n-1 dimensionality ensures immanence (no need for external/higher-dimensional space) | Ch. 5, pp. 77-82 |
| Symmetry-breaking cascade | Progressive elimination of symmetries producing increasingly differentiated entities. Klein's Erlangen program applied ontologically: topology → differential → affine → projective → metric geometry | Replaces essences as the mechanism of individuation. Chemical species individuated by successive symmetry-breaking of electron orbital geometry. Biological species by progressive specification of developmental pathways. THE mechanism connecting virtual (topological) to actual (metric) | Ch. 5, pp. 79-82; Ch. 6, pp. 96-100 |
| Divergent actualization | One virtual structure (distribution of singularities) produces multiple, qualitatively different actual entities without resemblance between cause and effect | The ontological principle that defeats essentialism: identity is not given by a fixed essence but produced by a historical process of individuation. The SAME singularity (minimum of potential energy) is actualized as a soap bubble AND a crystal AND a geodesic — no shared essence, only shared topology | Ch. 6, pp. 93-96 |
| Plane of consistency | Cosmic limit of maximal deterritorialization/decoding where all assemblages' diagrams enter mutual relations of exteriority | Asymptotic limit, never reached. Where all virtual structures coexist and interact WITHOUT the mediation of actual entities. D&G's "Body without Organs" at maximum. Three kinds of lines run through assemblages toward this limit: molar, molecular, lines of flight | Ch. 5, pp. 84-88 |
| Nested assemblages | Assemblages compose recursively: persons → communities → organizations → cities → nation-states, where emergent properties at each level become components at the next | Replaces reified generalities ("the Market," "the State") with populations of individual entities at each scale. Each level has its own emergent properties irreducible to lower levels, but composed from them. Causation runs both bottom-up (emergence) AND top-down (constraint) | Ch. 1, pp. 10-18 |
| Minor vs Major science | Minor (chemistry, metallurgy): follows phenomena, uses reference models, operates problematically (seeking solutions to problems). Major (physics): interrogates with axioms, uses universal theorems, operates theorematically | NOT a fixed classification but a *variable parameter setting*. Any field can operate in minor or major mode. The minor/major distinction maps to coding: minor = decoded (flexible, adaptive), major = coded (rigid, axiomatic). DeLanda argues chemistry has been *misrepresented* as having become major | Ch. 4, pp. 57-70 |
| Problems vs Solutions | Problems are defined by the virtual structure of possibility spaces (distribution of singularities). Solutions are actual trajectories/entities that satisfy the problem's conditions. Problems SURVIVE their solutions | The problem of progressive differentiation (symmetry-breaking cascade) persists whether actualized in fluid dynamics, chemical speciation, or linguistic evolution. This gives problems objective ontological status independent of human minds — assemblages confront and solve problems throughout their lives | Ch. 7, pp. 106-120 |

## Theoretical & Methodological Implications

**Method**: DeLanda employs what might be called *reconstructive systematization* — taking concepts from D&G's deliberately rhizomatic and anti-systematic prose and rendering them in analytic philosophical idiom with explicit definitions, formal analogies, and empirical case studies [throughout]. This is itself a methodological statement: assemblage theory CAN be systematized without betraying its content, contra readings that treat D&G's obscurity as constitutive of their philosophy.

The method combines three philosophical traditions: (1) Deleuzian ontology (virtual/actual, singularities, individuation); (2) analytic philosophy of science (Van Fraassen's state-space approach, Kitcher's practice structures, Shapere's domains) [Ch. 4, pp. 60-62; Ch. 7, pp. 107-110]; (3) realist ontology (Bhaskar's dispositional realism, rejection of social constructivism and linguistic idealism) [Ch. 6, pp. 91-93]. The fusion is deliberate: DeLanda argues that D&G's ontology is fundamentally realist (compatible with scientific practice) rather than postmodernist (incompatible with it) [Introduction, pp. 5-6].

**Parametrization as method**: The most distinctive methodological contribution is the systematic conversion of binary distinctions into continuously variable parameters. Where D&G sometimes write as if strata and assemblages were different kinds of entity, DeLanda treats them as different settings on the same parameters [Introduction, pp. 3-5]. This transforms qualitative philosophical distinctions into quantitative (or at least ordinal) ones: any assemblage can be *placed* on the territorialization and coding axes, yielding a phase space of possible assemblage configurations. The method implies that philosophical concepts should be constructed as *variables* with ranges, not as *types* with fixed properties [Ch. 1, pp. 15-18].

**Anti-essentialism via individuation**: The book replaces the concept of natural kinds (species defined by shared essence) with *individuation processes* driven by symmetry-breaking cascades [Ch. 6, pp. 89-105]. This methodological commitment means that explanation requires historical narrative (how did this entity come to be?) rather than subsumption under law (what class does it belong to?). Essences are replaced by topological invariants; classification by genealogy; being by becoming [Ch. 6, pp. 93-100].

**Implication for social science**: The nested assemblage framework implies that reified generalities ("the Market," "the State," "Society") must be eliminated and replaced by populations of concrete individual entities at each scale [Ch. 1, pp. 14-15]. This is a flat ontology that denies privileged macro-entities while acknowledging genuine emergence at every scale. Social explanation must trace causal mechanisms through specific nested levels, not invoke macro-categories as if they were actors [Ch. 1, pp. 10-34].
