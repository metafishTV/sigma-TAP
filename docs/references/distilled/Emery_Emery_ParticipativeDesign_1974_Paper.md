# Fred & Merrelyn Emery (1974/2017/2022) — Participative Design: Work and Community Life

**Source**: Fred Emery and Merrelyn Emery, "Participative Design: Work and Community Life," originally published 1974, revised through 2017 (Merrelyn Emery editor), reprinted 2022. The foundational and most detailed exposition of the Participative Design Workshop methodology.

**Core contribution**: The most comprehensive treatment of the PDW methodology in the OST literature. Contains the full structural analysis of DP1 and DP2, the complete six criteria with bidirectional/monotonic scoring system, the skills matrix methodology, the three-phase PDW process, the three DP2 variants, and critical distinctions (self-managing vs autonomous, genotypical vs phenotypical, laissez-faire vs DP2). This is the paper that practitioners use as their primary reference for conducting organizational redesign.

---

## 1. DP1: The Full Structural Analysis

### The Building Block: One Person-Shift Unit

Under DP1, the fundamental unit of organizational design is one person doing one task for one shift. This is the "redundancy of parts" principle made concrete: each person IS a part, replaceable by any other person trained in the same narrow function. The organization is designed so that parts fail independently and can be swapped without affecting the system's overall structure.

### The Hierarchical Control Structure

DP1 produces a specific structural form:

**S2 -> S1 -> People/Tasks**

Where S2 is senior management (strategic control), S1 is supervisory management (operational control), and People/Tasks are the workers performing fragmented subtasks. Responsibility for coordination and control is located at S1 -- one level above where work is done. S1's function is to ensure that the parts (people) perform their assigned subtasks in the correct sequence, at the correct rate, to the correct standard.

This structure has several necessary consequences:

1. **Information loss at each relay**: Information passing from workers to S1 to S2 is filtered, compressed, and distorted at each level. S1 filters what S2 needs to know (amplifying some signals, attenuating others). S2 filters what S1 should communicate downward. The organization operates on progressively degraded information as it moves from the work face to strategic decision-making.

2. **Communication as weapon**: Under DP1, information is a competitive resource. Hoarding information provides leverage; sharing information reduces advantage. Communication between units becomes strategic rather than cooperative -- each unit manages its information flow to maximize its position relative to other units and relative to the supervisory hierarchy.

3. **"Gang warfare" at any level**: The competitive dynamics of DP1 operate at every organizational level, not just the shop floor. Departments compete with departments, divisions with divisions, executives with executives. The structural form produces the same pathological dynamics regardless of the content of the work or the education of the workers. This is a genotypical claim: the pathology is in the STRUCTURE, not in the people.

### The Shadow Organization

Under DP1, a shadow organization inevitably forms. This shadow organization consists of:

- **Dargs** (derived from "day's work" -- informally negotiated output levels): Workers collectively establish production norms that protect the group from management exploitation. These norms are invisible to the formal organization but powerfully constraining.

- **Cliques organized around trust bases NOT task interdependencies**: The shadow organization's social structure does not mirror the formal organization chart. Instead, cliques form along pre-existing lines of trust, shared background, shared grievance, or mutual protection. These trust-based cliques may cut across formal departmental boundaries or may fragment a single department into hostile subgroups.

- **The filter/amplifier system of "us" vs "them"**: The shadow organization operates a perceptual filter that classifies all organizational events as either threatening ("them" -- management, other departments, external forces) or protective ("us" -- the clique, the darg group, the informal alliance). This filter amplifies threat signals and attenuates cooperative signals, producing a progressively adversarial organizational climate.

The shadow organization is not a failure of DP1 -- it is DP1's necessary complement. Where the formal organization fragments tasks and atomizes workers, the shadow organization provides the social infrastructure that makes the work bearable. But it does so pathologically: the trust bases are defensive, the communication is strategic, and the norms (dargs) are oriented toward protection rather than production.

## 2. DP2: The Full Structural Analysis

### The Group as Fundamental Unit

Under DP2, the fundamental unit is not the individual person-shift but the **group jointly responsible for a whole task**. The group is responsible for:

1. **All tasks** comprising the whole meaningful unit of work
2. **All interdependencies** between those tasks -- sequencing, quality coordination, resource allocation
3. **Monitoring own members** -- performance, attendance, skill development, mutual support
4. **Organizing to cope with variations** -- shift changes, absences, demand fluctuations, equipment failures
5. **Meeting agreed goals** -- production targets, quality standards, delivery schedules, safety requirements

### Self-Managing, NOT Autonomous

This distinction is critical and extensively argued in the paper. Self-managing groups are:

- **Interdependent** with the larger organizational context: they transact with management (who provides boundary management, resources, environmental intelligence), with other teams (who handle adjacent tasks, shared resources, sequential dependencies), and with the environment (customers, suppliers, regulators).

- **Accountable** for meeting negotiated goals: DP2 does not eliminate accountability but relocates it. Instead of individual accountability to a supervisor (DP1), there is group accountability to the organizational context (DP2). Goals are negotiated, not imposed, but once agreed they are binding.

- **Bounded** by the organizational mission: the group's autonomy extends to HOW it accomplishes its goals, not to WHAT goals it pursues. The what is negotiated; the how is self-determined.

Autonomous groups, by contrast, would determine their own goals independently of organizational context. This is not DP2 but a different (and generally unworkable) organizational form. The paper is emphatic: calling DP2 groups "autonomous" invites both misunderstanding (critics assume no accountability) and misapplication (practitioners remove all constraints and produce laissez-faire).

### Role Fluidity

Under DP2, there are no stable roles within the group. This is a structural claim, not merely a recommendation:

- **Temporary leadership moves around**: Whoever has the most relevant knowledge or skill for the current task takes the lead. Leadership is functional, not positional. It shifts continuously as the task demands change.

- **In emergencies, call on the person with most knowledge**: The group's emergency response is not to escalate to a supervisor (DP1) but to mobilize its own most capable member. This requires that members know each other's capabilities -- the skills matrix is the formal record of this knowledge.

- **No fixed division of labor within the group**: Members rotate across tasks, both to develop multiskilling (redundancy of functions) and to prevent the re-emergence of DP1 dynamics (one person "owning" a task and becoming a de facto supervisor).

## 3. The Six Criteria: Complete Formulations with Scoring

### Criteria 1-3: Optimization Criteria (Bidirectional Scoring)

These three criteria are scored on a scale from **-5 to +5**, where 0 is the optimum. The bidirectional scoring is structurally significant: BOTH too much and too little are pathological. This is not a simple "more is better" logic.

**Criterion 1: Adequate Elbow Room for Decision-Making**

- Score -5: No autonomy. Every decision made by supervisor. Worker is pure instrument. This is classical DP1 at its most extreme.
- Score 0: Optimal autonomy. Sufficient scope for judgment and decision-making within one's competence. The worker can exercise skill and discretion.
- Score +5: Total autonomy. No boundaries, no guidance, no coordination. This is laissez-faire, not freedom. The worker is abandoned, not empowered.

Under DP1, this criterion is structurally degraded: S1 makes the decisions, workers execute. The DP1 "solution" to poor performance is to further restrict autonomy (more rules, closer supervision), which further degrades the criterion, producing a downward spiral.

**Criterion 2: Opportunity for Learning on an Ongoing Basis**

- Score -5: No learning. Completely routinized work, no variation, no challenge, no skill development. The worker's capabilities atrophy.
- Score 0: Optimal learning. Continuous growth, appropriate challenge, feedback that enables improvement. The worker develops over time.
- Score +5: Overwhelming complexity. So much novelty that nothing is learned -- constant disorientation without consolidation. Change without integration.

Under DP1, learning is structurally blocked: narrow task specialization prevents exposure to new skills; supervisory control prevents experimentation; and additional training provided under DP1 is wasted because workers have no opportunity to practice new skills in their restricted roles.

**Criterion 3: An Optimal Level of Variety**

- Score -5: Total monotony. The same task, the same way, indefinitely. Psychological withdrawal and physical pathology result.
- Score 0: Optimal variety. Sufficient diversity to maintain engagement and cognitive alertness. The work holds attention without overwhelming.
- Score +5: Chaos. So much variety that no coherence emerges -- every moment is different, nothing connects, no skill deepens. This is not stimulation but fragmentation.

### Criteria 4-6: Maximization Criteria (Monotonic Scoring)

These three criteria are scored on a scale from **0 to 10**, where higher is always better. There is no ceiling -- the more of these, the better. Unlike criteria 1-3, these criteria REQUIRE DP2 structure. They are emergent group properties, not individually designable.

**Criterion 4: Mutual Support and Respect**

- Score 0: No mutual support. Each person for themselves. Competition, blame, indifference. The group provides no social infrastructure.
- Score 10: Deep mutual support. Members care about each other's wellbeing, cover for each other's weaknesses, celebrate each other's strengths. The group IS a support system.

Under DP1, mutual support is structurally undermined: competition for supervisory approval, individual performance measurement, and the shadow organization's defensive clique structure all prevent genuine mutual support. Workers may form defensive alliances, but these are "us vs them" solidarity, not productive mutual support.

**Criterion 5: Meaningfulness / Social Contribution**

- Score 0: No meaningfulness. The work contributes to nothing the worker values. It is pure instrumentality -- done for wages, not for purpose.
- Score 10: Deep meaningfulness. The work contributes to outcomes the worker cares about -- community wellbeing, environmental health, human development, valued products or services.

Under DP1, meaningfulness is structurally degraded by task fragmentation: each worker performs a small subtask whose relationship to the whole product/service/outcome is invisible. The worker cannot see the contribution because the contribution has been divided into invisible fragments.

**Criterion 6: A Desirable Future**

- Score 0: No desirable future. The current work leads nowhere -- no growth, no development, no career path, no expanding horizon. A dead end.
- Score 10: A richly desirable future. The current work opens pathways to valued future states -- skill development, career advancement, increasing scope, growing contribution.

### How DP1 Degrades Each Criterion

The paper provides a systematic structural analysis of how DP1 degrades each criterion:

| Criterion | DP1 Structural Mechanism | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (Autonomy) | S1 holds decision authority | Worker = instrument |
| 2 (Learning) | Narrow specialization + no practice opportunity | Deskilling spiral |
| 3 (Variety) | One person, one task, one shift | Monotony |
| 4 (Mutual support) | Individual competition + blame culture | Defensive cliques |
| 5 (Meaningfulness) | Task fragmentation | Invisible contribution |
| 6 (Desirable future) | Fixed role, limited mobility | Dead end |

### How DP2 Enables Each Criterion

| Criterion | DP2 Structural Mechanism | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (Autonomy) | Group holds coordination authority | Scope for judgment |
| 2 (Learning) | Multiskilling + rotation + whole task | Continuous growth |
| 3 (Variety) | Multiple tasks within whole task | Engaged attention |
| 4 (Mutual support) | Shared accountability + group self-management | Genuine care |
| 5 (Meaningfulness) | Whole task visible | Contribution seen |
| 6 (Desirable future) | Growing skills + career development + expanding scope | Open horizon |

## 4. Genotypical vs Phenotypical: The Critical Distinction

The paper's most polemical section attacks the "alphabet soup" of managerial interventions that claim to improve organizational life without changing the design principle. These are ALL phenotypical changes -- they alter surface features while leaving the genotypical structure (DP1) intact:

- **Job enlargement**: Adding more tasks to a DP1 role. Still one person, still supervised, still no group self-management. May improve variety (criterion 3) marginally but does not touch mutual support, meaningfulness, or desirable future.

- **Job enrichment**: Adding more decision-making to a DP1 role. May improve autonomy (criterion 1) marginally but within a structure that still locates coordination responsibility above the worker.

- **Rest pauses**: Ameliorating the symptoms of monotony without addressing the cause. The work is still monotonous; the pauses provide temporary relief.

- **Team building**: Attempting to improve relationships within a DP1 structure. The structural incentives (competition, individual accountability, supervisory control) undermine any relational gains. Team building under DP1 is "painting the cage a nicer color."

- **Matrix organizations**: Creating dual reporting lines. The paper is emphatic: matrix organizations do NOT produce DP2. They produce confusion, competing loyalties, and -- critically -- laissez-faire in the gaps between the two hierarchies. Workers caught between two DP1 bosses experience neither the clarity of single DP1 nor the self-management of DP2.

- **"Open culture" initiatives**: Removing visible hierarchy markers (corner offices, reserved parking, dress codes) without changing the underlying coordination structure. The hierarchy is invisible but intact.

All these interventions are phenotypical because they leave the GENOTYPE unchanged: responsibility for coordination remains located one level above where work is done (DP1). The only genotypical change is to relocate coordination responsibility to the people doing the work (DP2). Everything else is surface rearrangement.

**Matrix and team building specifically confirmed to create laissez-faire, NOT DP2**: The paper identifies matrix organization and team building as particularly dangerous because they are PERCEIVED as DP2 ("we have teams, we have shared authority, we are democratic") while being structurally laissez-faire ("no one knows who is responsible, decisions fall through the cracks, the loudest voice dominates"). This misidentification poisons the well for genuine DP2 interventions by creating the impression that "we tried self-management and it didn't work."

## 5. The Three-Phase PDW: Detailed Process

### Phase 1: Analysis

**Briefing**: Facilitator presents the six criteria and the DP1 design principle. This is education, not persuasion -- the facilitator explains the theoretical framework and lets workers apply it to their own experience.

**Group work on matrices**: Workers, organized in groups of 6-8, analyze their current work using several matrices:

1. **Six criteria assessment**: Each worker scores their current work on all six criteria. These scores are pooled and discussed. The typical result under DP1: criteria 1-3 score negative (too little autonomy, learning, variety), criteria 4-6 score low (insufficient mutual support, meaningfulness, desirable future). This produces the diagnostic -- a WORKER-GENERATED assessment that DP1 is failing.

2. **Task analysis**: Workers map all the tasks involved in their area of work, identifying interdependencies, sequences, and skill requirements. This produces the raw material for Phase 2 redesign.

3. **Skills matrix**: Workers assess their own and each other's skill levels across all identified tasks. The scoring system:
   - **0**: Cannot perform this task
   - **x**: Can perform this task adequately
   - **xx**: Can perform this task at high proficiency and can train others

### Phase 2: Change

**Briefing**: Facilitator presents the DP2 design principle. Workers now have both the diagnostic (Phase 1) and the target (DP2) and proceed to redesign.

**Redesign process**: Workers design their own DP2 structure. This involves:

1. **Defining the whole task**: What is the complete meaningful unit of work that a self-managing group should take responsibility for? This is the most critical design decision -- too narrow and the group is still doing fragmented subtasks; too broad and the group cannot coordinate effectively.

2. **Forming self-managing teams**: Who should be in which team? Based on task interdependencies (NOT existing friendship groups or managerial preferences), skills distribution, and geographical/temporal constraints.

3. **Skills matrix analysis**: Using the skills matrix from Phase 1 to identify vulnerabilities. The rule: **at least 2 people at high proficiency (xx) for each essential skill, plus backups developing toward proficiency**. This ensures the group has genuine redundancy of functions -- if one person is absent, the group can still perform all tasks.

4. **Training identification**: Where the skills matrix shows vulnerabilities (tasks with fewer than 2 people at xx), training plans are created. Training under DP2 is directly connected to practice opportunity -- unlike DP1, where training may be provided but practice opportunity is structurally blocked.

5. **Coordination mechanisms**: How will the group coordinate internally? Meeting schedules, communication protocols, decision-making processes. These are self-designed, not imposed.

### Phase 3: Practicalities

**Goals**: Must be comprehensive -- not just production targets but human, social, environmental, economic, and technical goals. Goals must be:
- **Measurable**: The group can track its own performance
- **Negotiated with management**: Not imposed from above (DP1) nor set unilaterally by the group (autonomous). The negotiation process itself is a DP2 practice.

**Warning**: Quantity-only goals create "gang" dynamics. If the only goal is output volume, the group will optimize for quantity at the expense of quality, safety, learning, and mutual support. The group becomes a production gang rather than a self-managing team. Comprehensive goals prevent this by requiring the group to balance multiple objectives.

**Training**: Specific training plans for skills matrix vulnerabilities. Timelines, resources, and practice opportunities.

**Career path**: Under DP2, career development changes. Instead of climbing a supervisory ladder (DP1), workers develop breadth (multiskilling) and depth (mastery) within and across teams. Career paths must be designed to make this visible and valued.

**Essential completion**: The first three tasks -- six criteria assessment, skills matrix construction, and initial DP2 design -- MUST be completed within the workshop. If these are deferred to "later," they will not happen. The workshop's intensive format creates the conditions for genuine engagement that cannot be replicated in normal organizational routines (which are structured by DP1 and will reassert DP1 dynamics).

## 6. Three DP2 Variants

The paper identifies three structural variants of DP2, each suited to different work characteristics:

### Variant (a): Full Multiskilled

All team members learn all tasks. Complete redundancy of functions. Any member can perform any task. The team is maximally flexible and resilient.

**Suited to**: Stable, well-defined work with a bounded set of tasks. Manufacturing, operations, maintenance. Where the whole task is repeatable and skill sets are learnable by all members.

### Variant (b): Non-Multiskilled

Team members maintain specialist skills but share coordination responsibility. Control remains with the individual specialist for their area of expertise; coordination is shared across the group.

**Suited to**: Work requiring deep specialist knowledge that cannot feasibly be replicated across all members. Research teams, clinical teams, professional service firms. The group coordinates its specialists rather than requiring each specialist to become a generalist.

### Variant (c): Overlapping Project Teams

Team membership is fluid. Workers belong to multiple teams based on project requirements. Teams form, accomplish a discrete task, and dissolve. Coordination comes from overlapping membership (shared individuals bridge different teams).

**Suited to**: Unstable, project-based work where the composition of the "whole task" changes frequently. R&D, consulting, creative industries. Where no permanent team structure can track the shifting demands.

**All three variants are mixable**: A single large organization may use variant (a) in its manufacturing operations, variant (b) in its R&D laboratories, and variant (c) in its project management function. The design principle (DP2) is constant; the structural expression varies with work characteristics.

## 7. Self-Managing Groups: Structural Requirements

### Minimum Size: 4 Members

The paper provides structural justification for a minimum group size of 4:

- **2 members**: Too close. The dyad creates intense dependency. If one member is absent or fails, the other is alone. Conflict between two people has no mediator. The relationship is too fragile for self-management.

- **3 members**: Shifting coalitions. The triad naturally forms 2-against-1 coalitions that rotate. This produces instability -- each member is alternately included and excluded. The coalition dynamics consume energy that should go to task performance.

- **4 members**: The minimum stable self-managing unit. Four members provide enough redundancy, enough perspective diversity, and enough coalition complexity to sustain genuine self-management. Coalitions are possible but not dominant; absence of one member still leaves a viable group.

### No Fixed Upper Size

The paper sets no maximum group size, arguing that the appropriate size depends on the whole task. However, practical constraints (communication overhead, coordination complexity) typically limit effective self-managing groups to 8-15 members.

### Group Controls Its Own Membership

A critical DP2 feature: the group has some degree of control over who is a member. This does not mean absolute veto power over hiring/firing, but it means:
- The group participates in selecting new members
- The group can identify members who are not contributing and address this internally
- The group manages its own membership dynamics (integration of new members, development of skills, addressing interpersonal difficulties)

Without membership control, the group is not truly self-managing -- it is a collection of individuals assigned from above (DP1) who happen to share a workspace.

## 8. The Laissez-Faire Warning

This is one of the paper's most emphatic arguments:

**Removing the supervisor without genuine self-management understanding = laissez-faire, NOT DP2.**

If an organization removes the supervisory layer (S1) without first educating workers in DP2 principles and conducting a genuine PDW, the result is NOT self-management but chaos. Workers who have been trained by DP1 to depend on supervisory direction, compete with each other, and protect their narrow task domains will not spontaneously self-manage. They will:

1. **Flounder**: No one knows who is responsible for coordination
2. **Regress to dominance hierarchies**: The loudest, most aggressive, or most politically connected members take de facto control -- producing informal DP1 without the formal structure
3. **Lose productivity**: Without either formal coordination (DP1) or self-managed coordination (DP2), work falls through the cracks
4. **Blame "self-management"**: The failure is attributed to the concept rather than to the absence of genuine structural change

**The conceptual knowledge requirement**: Groups MUST have conceptual knowledge of design principles or the design regresses toward DP1. This is not optional education but a structural necessity. Without understanding WHY they are self-managing (the six criteria, the design principles, the skills matrix methodology), workers cannot sustain DP2 under pressure. When stress increases (production demands, equipment failures, interpersonal conflict), groups without conceptual understanding will default to the DP1 patterns they have internalized over years of bureaucratic experience.

This is why the PDW includes Phase 1 (education + analysis) BEFORE Phase 2 (redesign). The education is not context-setting -- it is structural foundation.

## 9. Goals Under DP2

### Comprehensive Goal Setting

Goals under DP2 must be comprehensive across five dimensions:

1. **Human**: Worker wellbeing, skill development, job satisfaction, health and safety
2. **Social**: Team cohesion, mutual support, community contribution
3. **Environmental**: Ecological impact, resource efficiency, sustainability
4. **Economic**: Productivity, cost efficiency, revenue contribution
5. **Technical**: Quality, reliability, innovation, process improvement

No single dimension is sufficient. The paper warns specifically against quantity-only or economic-only goals.

### The "Gang" Warning

When goals are exclusively quantitative (units produced, revenue generated, targets hit), the self-managing group becomes a production gang:

- **Internal competition** replaces mutual support: members compete for credit, output attribution, performance bonuses
- **Cutting corners**: Quality, safety, environmental standards, and member wellbeing are sacrificed for quantity
- **Exclusion of weaker members**: The group ejects or marginalizes members who cannot match the pace, rather than developing their skills
- **Short-term focus**: Long-term development (skill building, process improvement, member growth) is sacrificed for immediate output

Gang dynamics are not DP2 -- they are a pathological form that shares DP2's structural features (self-managing group, no supervisor) but lacks DP2's comprehensive goals and values. The structural similarity makes gangs hard to distinguish from genuine DP2 groups on organizational charts, but the lived experience is radically different.

### Goal Negotiation

Goals are negotiated between the self-managing group and management. This negotiation is a DP2 practice:

- **Management's role**: Provide organizational context (what does the business need?), resource constraints (what is available?), environmental intelligence (what is changing?). Management does NOT dictate goals.
- **Group's role**: Provide operational knowledge (what is achievable?), identify development needs (what training is required?), propose comprehensive goals across all five dimensions. The group does NOT set goals unilaterally.
- **Negotiation process**: Iterative, based on mutual respect and shared information. Both parties have legitimate interests and expertise. The outcome is a shared commitment, not a directive.

## 10. Industrial Relations

The paper addresses the industrial relations implications of DP1-to-DP2 transition:

### Draft Agreement Requirements

The transition from DP1 to DP2 requires a formal agreement that precisely relocates coordination and control responsibility. This agreement must:

1. **Specify what responsibilities move to the group**: Scheduling, quality control, task allocation, member development, performance monitoring, variation management
2. **Specify what responsibilities remain with management**: Boundary management, resource provision, environmental intelligence, inter-team coordination, strategic direction
3. **Specify the negotiation process for goals**: How goals are set, reviewed, and modified
4. **Specify dispute resolution**: How conflicts between teams, or between teams and management, are addressed

### Corrupted Terms Warning

The paper warns against using terms that have been corrupted by DP1 usage:

- **"Self managing teams"**: The word "teams" has been so extensively used in DP1 contexts (team building, team leaders, team meetings -- all within DP1 structures) that it carries DP1 connotations. Using it invites misunderstanding.
- **"Empowerment"**: Implies that power is a gift from management to workers. Under DP2, responsibility is structurally relocated, not bestowed. The language of empowerment preserves the assumption that management is the source of authority.
- **"Autonomous"**: Implies independence from organizational context. DP2 groups are self-managing but interdependent, not autonomous.

## 11. Bureaucratic Structures and Mental Illness

The paper references de Guerre et al (2008) on DP1's implication in epidemic levels of mental illness:

DP1 structures are not merely inefficient -- they produce measurable psychological harm. The structural degradation of the six criteria (especially autonomy, learning, meaningfulness, and mutual support) creates conditions that are clinically associated with depression, anxiety, burnout, and related disorders. This is not a claim about individual vulnerability but about structural pathogenesis: DP1 structures produce mental illness at population scale, regardless of individual resilience or coping strategies.

The implication is that organizational design is a public health issue. DP1 organizations externalize their psychological costs onto the healthcare system, families, and communities -- the same externalization mapped in de Guerre's "Tele and Tinny" spiral.

## Key Concepts for sigma-TAP Integration

### 1. Shadow Organization = Pathological Topology Under DP1

The shadow organization (dargs, trust-based cliques, "us vs them" filtering) is the informal network that forms under DP1 conditions. Its critical feature: it is organized around pre-existing trust bases, NOT task interdependencies. In sigma-TAP: when groups form under DP1 conditions (hierarchical coordination, supervisor-dependent), their junction structure should reflect pre-existing similarity (homophily -- agents connect to agents with similar type_sets or trust histories) rather than functional complementarity (agents connect to agents with complementary type_sets that would enable productive cross-metathesis). This homophilic topology degrades innovation capacity because it produces absorptive cross-metathesis (agents reinforcing what they already know) rather than novel cross-metathesis (agents combining different knowledge into new types). The shadow organization is the structural form that demetathesis takes at the group level.

### 2. Six Criteria Scoring = Agent Group Health Diagnostic

The bidirectional scoring (-5 to +5) for criteria 1-3 maps to the user's concept of optimal tension. Too much elbow room (score +5, laissez-faire) is as pathological as too little (score -5, domination). Too much learning demand (+5, overwhelming complexity) is as harmful as too little (-5, stagnation). Too much variety (+5, chaos) is as destructive as too little (-5, monotony). The optimum at 0 is a dynamic balance -- the sweet spot of the user's annular distribution (deadzone at center where nothing happens, peak at sweet-spot radius, decay at extremes). The monotonic scoring (0-10) for criteria 4-6 maps to maximization criteria that require DP2 structure and cannot be achieved by tuning individual parameters -- they are emergent group properties.

### 3. Skills Matrix = Type_Set Overlap Analysis

The xx/x/0 scoring maps directly to proficiency levels within type_sets:
- **xx** = type present AND well-integrated (the agent can exercise this type fluently and teach it)
- **x** = type present but not fully integrated (the agent can exercise this type adequately)
- **0** = type absent (the agent cannot exercise this capability)

The redundancy rule -- "at least 2 people at high proficiency (xx) for each essential skill, plus backups" -- translates to a minimum redundancy of functions for group viability. In sigma-TAP: a group needs sufficient type_set overlap to sustain absorptive cross-metathesis (maintaining existing capabilities through mutual reinforcement) but enough diversity to enable novel cross-metathesis (generating new types through combination of different capabilities). The skills matrix is the diagnostic instrument for this balance. A group where all agents have identical type_sets is maximally redundant but innovation-dead; a group where no agents share types is maximally diverse but coordination-impossible.

### 4. Three DP2 Variants = Three Possible Group Topologies

The three DP2 variants map to three structural configurations of agent groups in sigma-TAP:

**(a) Full multiskilled** = high type_set overlap across all group members, dense reciprocal L12/L21 traffic, any agent can perform any group task. This produces maximum resilience (loss of any agent does not disable the group) but requires investment in broad skill development. The group's innovation capacity comes from depth of shared understanding enabling subtle recombination, not from diversity of inputs.

**(b) Non-multiskilled** = specialist agents with distinct type_sets, shared coordination but individual control within specialties. Lower type_set overlap, strategic L12/L21 exchanges focused on coordination rather than skill transfer. Innovation capacity comes from diversity -- combining different specialist knowledge into novel configurations. Vulnerability: loss of a specialist may disable a capability.

**(c) Overlapping project teams** = fluid group membership, temporary groupings for discrete tasks. Topology changes continuously as agents join and leave project teams. Innovation capacity comes from cross-pollination -- agents carry knowledge between teams. Vulnerability: no stable group identity, coordination overhead from constant reconfiguration.

All three are structurally valid DP2 configurations. In sigma-TAP, the simulation should be capable of producing all three as emergent topologies under different conditions (work stability, skill distribution, task interdependency patterns).

### 5. Laissez-Faire as Structural Failure Mode = Demetathesis

Removing DP1 without establishing DP2 = demetathesis. The group loses its coordination mechanism (supervisory hierarchy) but does not acquire a replacement (self-managing coordination). In sigma-TAP: if a group's topology is disrupted (key agent dies or disintegrates, environmental shock breaks established patterns) without replacement coordination emerging from within the group, the group enters the laissez-faire attractor. This is not a stable state but a degenerative one: without coordination, the group's metathetic activity declines, agents' temporal states regress (from established toward inertial), type_sets atrophy, and the group progressively loses the capacity to self-organize. Laissez-faire is the structural precondition for full disintegration.

The conceptual knowledge requirement has a modeling analogue: groups that have experienced DP2 (agents with DP2 patterns encoded in their interaction histories / L-matrix entries) can recover from disruption more readily than groups that have only known DP1 (agents whose interaction histories encode only hierarchical dependency). The DP2 experience provides a structural template that agents can re-enact; DP1 experience provides only a template for waiting for instructions that never come.

### 6. Comprehensive Goals = The Meaningfulness Criterion

Quantity-only goals = partial praxis that collapses into competitive hexis. The paper's "gang" warning maps directly to competitive L12/L21 dynamics that destroy mutual support. When the only measure is output, cross-metathesis becomes competitive rather than cooperative: agents optimize for individual contribution to the shared metric rather than for group development. In sigma-TAP: goal structure (what agents optimize for) should modulate the character of metathetic events. Comprehensive goals (multiple objectives balanced across dimensions) produce cooperative metathesis; narrow goals (single quantitative target) produce competitive metathesis that degrades to gang dynamics.

### 7. DP1 Deskilling Spiral = Type_Set Atrophy

Under DP1, unused skills degrade. The paper is explicit: narrow specialization causes skill atrophy in all areas outside the specialized task. Additional training is structurally wasted because the DP1 structure provides no opportunity to practice newly acquired skills. In sigma-TAP: agents under DP1 conditions (hierarchical coordination, restricted task assignment) should lose type_set diversity over time. Types that are never exercised in metathetic events should degrade -- either dropping from the type_set entirely or losing proficiency (the xx -> x -> 0 trajectory). This is a measurable DP1 pathology: groups under DP1 conditions should show declining type_set diversity over simulation time, while groups under DP2 conditions should show stable or increasing diversity.

### 8. Self-Managing NOT Autonomous = Interdependence Not Isolation

DP2 groups are not isolated -- they transact with the environment (L12/L22) and with other groups. Pure autonomy = isolation = death. In sigma-TAP: a self-managing group that ceases to transact with its environment (L12/L21 channels go silent, L22 signal is ignored) will die as surely as one that is dominated by a hierarchical controller. The group needs ongoing environmental transaction to maintain its adaptive capacity. Self-management means internal coordination is self-determined; it does NOT mean external transaction is eliminated. The L22 channel (environmental causal texture) is the lifeline that prevents self-managing groups from becoming closed systems -- which, per Bertalanffy and the 1965 Emery-Trist paper, is the definition of death.

### 9. Minimum Group Size = Structural Stability Threshold

The minimum-4 rule (2=dyad dependency, 3=shifting coalitions, 4=stable self-management) provides a structural constraint for group formation in sigma-TAP. When family groups / topology clusters emerge, they should require a minimum viable membership (4+) to sustain DP2 dynamics. Groups below this threshold should either merge with other groups or degrade to unstable coalition dynamics (the triad problem) or dyad dependency. This is a testable structural prediction: groups of 2-3 agents should show qualitatively different (and less stable) dynamics than groups of 4+.
