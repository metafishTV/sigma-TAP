## Core Argument

OST is a coherent, scientifically grounded framework for understanding and redesigning organizations as open systems. The framework rests on three interdependent pillars: (1) open systems ontology — every organization transacts with an environment possessing its own causal texture (L22), and this system-environment relationship is the primary determinant of organizational viability; (2) two genotypical design principles — DP1 (redundancy of parts: hierarchical coordination, responsibility located above work) and DP2 (redundancy of functions: self-managing coordination, responsibility located with workers), which are exhaustive and mutually exclusive for any unit of coordination, with laissez-faire as the degenerate case where neither operates; (3) six criteria for productive work — three optimization criteria (elbow room, continuous learning, variety) achievable under either DP, plus three maximization criteria (mutual support, meaningfulness, desirable future) that structurally require DP2 as emergent group properties.

These theoretical pillars yield two practical tools forming a mandatory two-stage sequence: the Search Conference (SC) for participative strategic planning — environment analyzed first, DP2 process throughout, 20-40 participants — followed by the Participative Design Workshop (PDW) for organizational redesign — workers themselves diagnose current design against the Six Criteria, then redesign their own structure toward DP2. The sequence is non-negotiable: environmental awareness (SC) must precede structural transformation (PDW), because organizations that restructure without environmental feedback produce internally coherent but maladapted designs.

The website traces OST's genealogy from the Tavistock Institute's socio-technical discoveries (Trist & Bamforth coal mining studies, 1951), through the Norwegian Industrial Democracy Project (1960s), to Fred and Merrelyn Emery's Australian developments (1970s-80s), and argues that neoliberalism arrested a global DP2 momentum by the mid-1980s. The current practitioner community — rooted in agile, DDD, and Team Topologies — represents an emerging revival. The site also positions DP2 as historically deep: Aboriginal and Islander cultures operated DP2 structures for cooperative culture across a continent, predating modern formalization by millennia.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Significance |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| Open system | Organization that exchanges energy, matter, information with environment; viability depends on environmental transaction | Foundational ontological claim — all OST derives from this |
| L11/L12/L21/L22 | Formal notation for system-environment dynamics: L11=internal, L22=environmental causal texture, L12=system→env, L21=env→system | Structural backbone of OST analysis |
| Environmental causal texture (L22) | Environment's own internal dynamics, operating under laws incommensurate with system's L11 | Core insight: L22 is not mere background but active, knowable, and determining |
| Two-layer environmental model | Wider environment (L22-dominant: global, societal, ecological) + immediate environment (L12/L21-dominant: stakeholders, markets) | SC methodology design: wider analyzed first, then immediate |
| DP1 (Redundancy of parts) | Coordination responsibility located one level above work; workers as replaceable parts with narrow skills | Produces bureaucracy, deskilling, dependency, slow adaptation |
| DP2 (Redundancy of functions) | Coordination responsibility located with workers; multiskilled self-managing teams share accountability | Produces democratic structure, continuous learning, rapid adaptation |
| DP exhaustiveness | DP1 and DP2 are the only two genotypical design principles; mutually exclusive for any single coordination relationship | No third option — binary structural choice for every unit of work |
| Laissez-faire | Absence of design principle — neither DP1 nor DP2 operates | NOT extreme DP2 but structural void producing chaos, conflict, dominance by loudest |
| Six criteria for productive work | 6 intrinsic motivators: (1) elbow room, (2) continuous learning, (3) variety, (4) mutual support/respect, (5) meaningfulness, (6) desirable future | Developed/tested over 40 years by Fred & Merrelyn Emery |
| Optimization vs maximization split | Criteria 1-3 optimized for balance (too much or too little possible); criteria 4-6 maximized (never enough) | Criteria 1-3 designable into individual jobs; criteria 4-6 require DP2 as emergent group properties |
| Search Conference (SC) | 2.5-day participative strategic planning; 20-40 participants; DP2 throughout; environment analyzed before system | Part 1 of two-stage model — strategic direction |
| Participative Design Workshop (PDW) | Organizational redesign by workers; 24-36 participants in groups of 6-8; diagnose via Six Criteria then redesign toward DP2 | Part 2 of two-stage model — structural transformation |
| Two-stage model | SC→PDW mandatory sequence: environmental awareness → structural transformation | Sequence is non-negotiable — restructuring without environmental feedback produces maladaptation |
| Joint optimization | Social + technical systems must be jointly optimized; neither alone suffices | "Holy grail" of organizational research — discovered through decades of socio-technical work |
| Socio-technical systems | Discovery (Trist & Bamforth, 1951) that social and technical systems co-determine outcomes | Genealogical foundation — coal mining studies demonstrated destruction of jointly-optimized traditional work groups |
| Action research | Tavistock methodology: workplace experiments rather than laboratory studies | OST's epistemological commitment — knowledge through practice, not abstraction |
| Unique designs | Custom hybrid workshops combining SC and PDW elements for specific contexts | Methodological flexibility within the two-stage framework |
| Neoliberalism as regression | Market fundamentalism + managerial prerogative reversed global DP2 momentum by mid-1980s | L22 counter-force — external ideological environment arresting organizational transformation |

## Theoretical & Methodological Implications

The website operates as **applied theory** — translating academic OST into practitioner-accessible form. Its method is advocacy-exposition: presenting the OST framework as complete, validated, and ready for adoption. The epistemological commitment is action research (knowledge through workplace experiment, confirmed through structural corroboration across contexts), and the ontology is open systems (organizations as L11 embedded in L22, not isolable units).

Three methodological claims are implicit: (1) **environmental primacy** — analysis of L22 must precede analysis of L11, reversing the typical organizational consulting approach; (2) **worker sovereignty** — the people who do the work are the experts on redesigning it, not external consultants; (3) **binary genotypical structure** — DP1 and DP2 are exhaustive, meaning all organizational pathology maps to either wrong DP choice or absence of DP (laissez-faire).

The Q&A with Merrelyn Emery adds a **biographical-reflexive** layer: the history of OST as lived experience, including personal anecdotes (Geelong road SC, Telecom PDW), the "hot water" from TV research, and the normative urgency about climate change. Her closing position — that both AI and OST are irrelevant without emissions stabilization — represents the L22 argument taken to its planetary limit.

The site's **genealogical claims** (thin red line from Faraday to Angyal to 1965 formalization; Aboriginal/Indigenous knowledge predating modern OST by millennia) position DP2 not as modern invention but as rediscovery of an empirically-observed structural optimum for cooperative systems in complex environments.
