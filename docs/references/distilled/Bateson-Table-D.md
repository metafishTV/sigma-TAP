# Ruesch & Bateson (1951) — Table D: The Specification of Networks at the Four Levels of Communication

**Source**: Jurgen Ruesch & Gregory Bateson, *Communication: The Social Matrix of Psychiatry* (New York: Norton, 1951), p. 277 (Conclusion chapter). Table D is the synoptic reference table for the entire four-level communication architecture. It accompanies Figure 4 (cone diagram of communication levels) and is described in detail across pp. 278–289.

**Core contribution**: Provides the complete structural specification of the four communication network levels across six dimensions: origin of messages, means of communication, channels, register (evaluation mode), and importance of interaction. This is the single most condensed representation of R&B's entire theoretical framework.

---

## Table Structure

The table cross-tabulates four levels (rows) against six structural dimensions (columns):

**Columns**: Origin of Messages | Means | Channels | Register | Importance of Interaction

**Rows**: I. Intrapersonal | II. Interpersonal | III. Group | IV. Cultural

---

## Level I: Intrapersonal ("Within one")

| Dimension | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Origin** | Self (one organism). Both origin and destination within the same organism. |
| **Means** | Proprioception (internal sensors: chemical, mechanical stimuli → feelings/sensations) and exteroception (surface sensors: light, sound, wave phenomena → information about self-environment relations). Propriotransmission (efferent nervous impulses to smooth muscles, humoral chemical pathways → regulation). Exterotransmission (striped muscles → action upon world, communication with others). |
| **Channels** | Neural, chemical, and other internal signals. The self-observer is always *totally participant*. |
| **Register** | The codification system can *never be examined* from within. Internal codification is the relation between neural/chemical signals and the events they represent. The possibilities of rearrangement are limited; the organism can somewhat predict its own reactions. |
| **Interaction** | Self-correction is the basic process, but correction of errors is *difficult if not impossible* because both origin and destination are within one organism. Complementarity between proprioception and exteroception: complete information would require combining both, but this is impossible — at any moment the organism specializes in one mode, failing to collect information available from the other. Pain may preclude external perceptiveness; violent external events may preclude awareness of pain or fatigue. |

**Structural principle**: The intrapersonal is a closed circuit. No external correction possible. The observer is fully embedded. The code cannot examine itself. Information is always partial due to the complementarity constraint.

---

## Level II: Interpersonal ("One to one")

| Dimension | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Origin** | Dyad. Two persons. Both origin and destination of messages *known* to senders and recipients. |
| **Means** | Language (verbal symbolization) together with nonverbal communication (more tenuous symbolisms). |
| **Channels** | Potentially equivalent parts — the directional flow characteristic of neural networks is *absent*. Potentialities for receiving, transmitting, and evaluating are equally divided. |
| **Register** | The observer of others must be *partially participating, partially observing*. When observing: bigger Gestalt; when participating: narrowed Gestalt. Both modes are parts of experience and complement each other, but complementation is never complete. At any moment the individual must specialize in one mode. |
| **Interaction** | Correction of information *is possible* (because origin and destination are known). But the individual can *never perceive himself perfectly in relation to others* — always a discrepancy between proprioceptive self-view and exteroceptive/social self-view. Cannot simultaneously hold proprioceptive self-picture and status-defined self-picture. Capacity and extent of network still within assessable range, but topology is undefined, making prediction difficult or impossible. |

**Structural principle**: The interpersonal introduces correction (error repair) because both ends of the communication are identified. But it also introduces an irreducible perceptual gap — the individual can never see himself as others see him at the same moment he sees himself from within. The participation-observation complementarity is formally identical to the proprioception-exteroception complementarity at Level I, but now externalized between persons.

---

## Level III: Group ("One to many" / "Many to one")

| Dimension | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Origin** | Multiple persons organized into a larger unit. Potentialities for receiving and transmitting are *unequally divided* among persons — specialization of function. |
| **Means** | Verbal and nonverbal processes (as at Level II) plus *new types of symbolization not ordinarily regarded as such* — the patterns of group organization themselves leave traces in individuals. The group in action possesses the information, not the individual. |
| **Channels** | Re-establishes directional flow (lost at Level II) through specialization. Only source *or* destination (not both) is typically known. Individuals may act as source/destination or merely as channels relaying messages. |
| **Register** | Two message types: (1) "One to many" = one-way flow center→periphery, reply delayed; the "one" transmits, the "many" receive. (2) "Many to one" = one-way flow toward center; progressive abstraction necessary because of limited receiver capacity; the "one" receives, the "many" transmit. Correction is *delayed* and possible only by short-cutting established pathways. |
| **Interaction** | Specialization makes the group as a whole *more predictable* than non-organized aggregates. But specialization implies *impoverished perception* for each individual. When two groups are in contact, each group's picture of itself and the other is *inflexible, stereotyped, and projective*. The completeness of information obtained by any individual decreases with every increase in complexity and differentiation. |

**Structural principle**: The group level introduces organization — unequal division of communicative function that creates structure but impoverishes individual perception. The group possesses information that no individual member possesses (emergence). Correction becomes possible only by short-circuiting established channels. The two message types (centrifugal and centripetal) correspond to Jakobs's egocentric and sociocentric subdivisions.

---

## Level IV: Cultural ("Many to many")

| Dimension | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Origin** | Unknown. Messages transmitted from many to many, but *sources and destinations are unknown*. The individual cannot recognize that messages travel in a network structure. |
| **Means** | The message is an *unstated description of their way of living*. People attribute no human origin to cultural messages but transmit them by living in accordance with their content, regarding it as "human nature." Vehicles include: language/linguistic systems, ethical premises, theories of man's relation to universe and fellow man, printed word, historical/mythological documents and monuments. |
| **Channels** | Potentialities for receiving and transmitting are *unascribed*. The cultural organization is beyond the reach of observation of the individual, who carries the cultural message implicitly in everyday actions. Being an infinitesimal part of the network, the individual's function as communication channel is overshadowed by intrapersonal and interpersonal events. |
| **Register** | Codification is entirely different from lower levels. At Levels I and II, codification is *atomistic* (separable events stand for separable events). At Level III, no atomism — organization as codification. At Level IV, the organization is beyond individual observation; the individual implicitly carries the message in daily life. People are in many cases *unaware* of being receivers or senders. |
| **Interaction** | Correction of information is *impossible* (because sources and destinations are unknown). Temporal and spatial limits of network not perceptible to participants, who also cannot perceive its topology. For participants, *predictability is minimal*. |

**Structural principle**: The cultural is the domain of the imperceptible. Messages are not recognized as messages. Origins and destinations are unknown. Correction is impossible. The individual is an infinitesimal relay in a system too large to perceive. This is the domain of L22 — processes in the environment itself that are among the determining conditions of exchange but are invisible to the exchanging parties.

## Cross-Level Patterns

### Correction Capacity
- L1: Impossible (closed circuit, no external check)
- L2: Possible (both ends identified)
- L3: Delayed (only via short-circuiting established channels)
- L4: Impossible (sources and destinations unknown)

The correction capacity follows a **non-monotonic arc**: impossible → possible → delayed → impossible. This means the interpersonal is the *only* level where direct, real-time error correction is fully available. The group delays it; the cultural makes it impossible again — but for entirely different reasons than the intrapersonal (intrapersonal: too close; cultural: too far).

### Codification Type
- L1: Neural/chemical signals (internal, non-examinable)
- L2: Language + nonverbal symbolism (atomistic, interpersonally shareable)
- L3: Organizational patterns as codification (non-atomistic, emergent)
- L4: Way of living as codification (implicit, carried unconsciously)

### Predictability
- L1: Somewhat predictable (limited rearrangement possibilities)
- L2: Difficult to predict (undefined topology despite assessable capacity)
- L3: More predictable as a whole (specialization creates structure)
- L4: Minimal predictability (limits and topology imperceptible)

Another **non-monotonic pattern**: moderate → low → higher → minimal.

### Information Completeness
Progressive impoverishment from Level I outward. At Level I, the organism has direct (if complementary-limited) access to its own states. At Level II, complementarity between participation and observation is never complete. At Level III, specialization impoverishes individual perception. At Level IV, the individual is unconscious of the network.

### Short Circuits
R&B identify a crucial mechanism across all levels: short circuits that bypass established channels. In large networks (group, cultural), interpersonal links are introduced to personalize mass communication (e.g., government emissaries). In small networks (intrapersonal, interpersonal), short circuits convey alarm signals that maintain network cohesion by warning of threatened dissolution (e.g., anxiety).

## Interaction and Self-Correction (Section IV of the Conclusion)

The Conclusion extends Table D with a section on interaction at each level. Key principles:

1. **Interaction always involves statements at two or more levels of abstraction**: statements about participating entities AND statements about the larger entity brought into being by interaction. Even person-thing relations involve interaction (self-correction from observing effects of action).

2. **Unpredictability is structural**: At any given instant, the individual does not yet have information about the effects of his current action. Predictions about his own later actions contain guesswork. If rigidly bound to his own guesses (ignoring later information), the larger system becomes rigid and incapable of self-correction.

3. **Interaction as ongoing self-correction**: Study of interaction = study of success or failure of self-correction. Concerned with ability to predict AND ability to modify action when predictions fail.

4. **Knowledge is dual**: Every message is simultaneously indicative (about the past) and imperative (implications for future action). Information and action have a dual relationship: goal-directed behavior is corrected by feedback, BUT action also *liberates codified information unavailable until action is in progress*. This practice-learning relation obtains at all levels.

5. **Destructive interaction** may result from: (a) incomplete information about self, others, or system; (b) discrepancies in evaluation of goals vs. instrumental actions (self-maximizing tendency destroying the larger system necessary for the self's existence); (c) self-destruction of smaller entity instrumental to survival of larger system. Purpose can only be discussed after delineating the system to whose maintenance the action contributes — requires an observer.

## Key Concepts for sigma-TAP Integration

1. **Non-monotonic correction arc**: L1 impossible → L2 possible → L3 delayed → L4 impossible. The only level of direct real-time correction is the interpersonal (L12/L21). This grounds why cross-metathesis is the critical dynamic — it's the only channel where error correction operates freely.

2. **L22 as imperceptible network**: At the cultural level, individuals are unconscious relays. They cannot identify sources, destinations, or network topology. Correction is impossible. This matches Emery & Trist's L22 — processes in the environment itself, invisible to the participants. The environment operates through people without their knowledge.

3. **Action liberates information**: Information unavailable until action is in progress. This is not merely feedback (which corrects action) but a deeper principle — praxis *generates* the very information that praxis needs. Maps directly to the praxitive deferral model: agents learn what they need by acting, not by observing.

4. **Complementarity constraint at every level**: At L1, proprioception/exteroception cannot be simultaneous. At L2, participation/observation cannot be simultaneous. At L3, source/destination knowledge is partial. At L4, awareness itself is impossible. Each level has its own structural complementarity that prevents total information.

5. **Group possesses information no individual possesses**: Codification at Level III requires intactness of organization as a whole. The information is in the *topology* not the *nodes*. Maps to the emergence principle in sigma-TAP — collective dynamics irreducible to individual agent states.

6. **Short circuits = alarm signals**: The mechanism for maintaining network cohesion against dissolution. In intrapersonal/interpersonal networks, these create anxiety as a cohesion-preserving signal. In group/cultural networks, these personalize mass communication. Maps to the overconservation detection and beneficial-alarm mechanisms.

7. **Self-maximizing tendency destroying necessary larger systems**: Destructive interaction where optimizing at one level destroys the conditions for that level's existence. The individual who maximizes his own position may destroy the group he needs. Directly maps to the demetathesis mechanism — praxis that undermines its own conditions.

8. **Indicative AND imperative**: Every message is simultaneously a statement about the past and an instruction for the future. No pure observation; no pure command. Maps to the dual nature of the practico-inert (sedimented past praxis that constrains future praxis).
