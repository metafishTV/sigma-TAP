# Levinas (1961) — Totality and Infinity: An Essay on Exteriority

**Source**: Emmanuel Levinas, *Totality and Infinity: An Essay on Exteriority*, trans. Alphonso Lingis (Pittsburgh: Duquesne University Press, 1969). Three excerpted sections: pp. 114–121 (Separation as Life), pp. 191–193 (Sensibility and the Face), pp. 220–232 (The Ethical Relation and Time).

**Core contribution**: Provides the philosophical infrastructure for understanding separation (L11), ipseity, the face as irreducible alterity, the facade/face distinction, radical vs. numerical multiplicity, corporeity as the ontological regime of will-exposed-to-the-Other, and time as praxitive deferral of consummation.

---

## Section A: Separation as Life (pp. 114–121)

### Enjoyment and Independence

Life does not consist in seeking and consuming fuel. It consists in *consummating* terrestrial and celestial nourishments. The dependence on what is not the self has a counterpart that nullifies it: "What we live from does not enslave us; we enjoy it." Need cannot be interpreted as simple lack (contra Plato) nor as pure passivity (contra Kant). The human being *thrives on* his needs; he is *happy for* his needs.

**The paradox of "living from something"**: Not mastery on one hand and dependence on the other, but *mastery in this dependence*. Living from... is the dependency that turns into sovereignty, into happiness — essentially egoist. Need is a *happy dependence*, capable of satisfaction "like a void which gets filled." What need lacks is its source of plenitude and wealth.

**Happiness as accomplishment**: Happiness exists in a soul *satisfied* and not in a soul that has extirpated its needs — "a castrated soul." Life is happiness, and therefore life is personal. The consumption of foods is the food of life.

### Ipseity — The Unicity of the I

"The personality of the person, the ipseity of the I, which is more than the particularity of the atom and of the individual, is the particularity of the happiness of enjoyment."

**Enjoyment accomplishes the atheist separation**: It deformalizes the notion of separation — not a cleavage made in the abstract, but "the existence at home with itself of an autochthonous I." The soul dwells in what is not itself, but acquires its own identity by this dwelling in the "other" — and not logically, by opposition to the other.

**The I existing without genus**: The unicity of the I does not merely consist in being found in one sample only (like the Eiffel Tower or the Mona Lisa) but in *existing without having a genus*, without being the individuation of a concept. "The ipseity of the I consists in remaining outside the distinction between the individual and the general." The refusal of the concept is not resistance to generalization but is the whole content of the I — it *is* interiority. This drives the being into the dimension of interiority. "It is at home with itself."

This "logically absurd structure of unicity" — non-participation in genus — is the very egoism of happiness. Happiness suffices to itself through the "not sufficing to oneself" proper to need. The self-sufficiency of enjoying measures the egoism or the ipseity of the Ego and the Same.

### Affectivity as Ipseity

What is termed an "affective state" does not have the dull monotony of a state but is "a vibrant exaltation in which dawns the self." The I is not the *support* of enjoyment — the I is the very *contraction* of sentiment, "the pole of a spiral whose coiling and involution is drawn by enjoyment: the focus of the curve is a part of the curve." Enjoyment comes into play precisely as a coiling, a movement toward oneself.

The breach of totality accomplished by the solitude of enjoyment is radical. When the Other's critical presence calls this egoism into question, it will not destroy its solitude. The solitude will be recognized in the concern for knowing (a problem of origin — inconceivable in a totality). Only the notion of *creation* (not causality) is commensurate with such a question, respecting both the absolute novelty of the I and its attachment to a principle.

### Need and Corporeity

Need is "the primary movement of the same" — dependence across time, not instantaneous betrayal. This temporal dependence opens the possibility "to break, by labor and by economy, the very thrust of the alterity upon which need depends." Distance between man and world constitutes the essence of need. "A being has detached itself from the world from which it still nourishes itself!"

The body is the articulation of this ambiguity. The body is not only the way a subject is reduced to slavery/dependence but also "a way of possessing and of working, of having time." The body is the very self-possession by which the I, liberated from the world by need, overcomes the destitution of this liberation.

Having recognized needs as material (capable of satisfaction), the I can turn to what it does not lack — opening to Desire (which is different from need: in need one can "sink teeth into the real"; in Desire there is no satiety, only an uncharted future). To be a body is to have time in the midst of the facts.

### Multiplicity — Radical vs. Numerical

If the I represents nothing further, is precisely a self, then multiplicity can only be produced if individuals "retain their secrecy." The relation that unites them must proceed "from one unto the other" — not be visible from outside. If entirely visible from an exterior point of view, the multiplicity would form a totality; individuals would participate in it; the bond would not have preserved multiplicity from addition.

**Pluralism is not a numerical multiplicity.** For pluralism to be realized "there must be produced in depth the movement from me to the other" — an attitude already specified as love or hatred, obedience or command, learning or teaching — that would not be a species of relationship in general. This means the movement from me to the other could not present itself as a theme to an objective gaze freed from confrontation with the other.

"Pluralism implies a radical alterity of the other, whom I do not simply conceive by relation to myself, but confront out of my egoism." The alterity of the Other is *in him* and is not relative to me; it reveals itself. But I access it from myself and from the society I maintain with him — not by quitting the relation to reflect on its terms.

## Section B: Sensibility and the Face (pp. 191–193)

### Vision, Light, and the Lateral

Vision moves into grasp. Vision opens upon a perspective, a horizon, describes a traversable distance, invites the hand. Forms call for grasp. "Vision is not a transcendence." It ascribes signification by the relations it makes possible — opening nothing that would be absolutely other, that is, in itself. Light conditions the relations between data; it makes possible the signification of objects that border one another. "It does not enable one to approach them face to face."

Space, instead of transporting beyond, simply ensures the condition for "the lateral signification of things within the same." To see is always to see on the horizon — and the vision that apprehends on the horizon does not encounter a being out of what is beyond all being.

### Facade vs. Face

Total alterity — where a being presents itself *out of itself* — does not shine forth in *form*. Beneath form, things conceal themselves. The surface can be transformed into an interior (melting metal, chopping wood). The depth of the thing is its matter, and the revelation of matter is essentially superficial.

**The facade**: Borrowed from architecture (perhaps the first of the fine arts). The facade exposes the thing which keeps its secret — "enclosed in its monumental essence and in its myth, in which it gleams like a splendor but does not deliver itself." It captivates by grace as by magic but *does not reveal itself*. Art endows things with facade — objects not only seen but "on exhibition." The darkness of matter denotes the state of a being that precisely has no facade. The beautiful consists in indifference, cold splendor, and silence.

**The face**: "If the transcendent cuts across sensibility... it cuts across the vision of forms and can be stated neither in terms of contemplation nor in terms of practice. It is the face; its revelation is speech. The relation with the Other alone introduces a dimension of transcendence, and leads us to a relation totally different from experience in the sensible sense of the term, relative and egoist."

## Section C: The Ethical Relation and Time (pp. 220–232)

### Subjectivity and Pluralism

Separation — effected concretely as habitation and economy — makes possible the relation with detached, absolute exteriority. This relation (metaphysics) is brought about primordially by the epiphany of the Other in the face. Separation opens between terms that are *absolute and yet in relation* — that absolve themselves from the relation they maintain.

**Radical multiplicity vs. numerical multiplicity**: The dimension of height from which the Metaphysical comes indicates "a sort of non-homogeneity of space, such that a radical multiplicity, distinct from numerical multiplicity, can here be produced." *Numerical multiplicity remains defenseless against totalization.* In mere disclosure (where being manifests, effectuates, reigns), the diverse is united under the panoramic look. Contemplation is absorbed into totality. The subjectivity of knowledge cannot break with this totality.

For multiplicity to be maintained, subjectivity must be produced that *cannot seek congruence with the being in which it is produced*. Being must flow toward an I infinitely "without running dry, burning without being consumed" — but this approach cannot be cognition (which absorbs). The impossibility of total reflection must be posited positively as "the surplus of the social relation, where the subjectivity remains in face of..."

The primordial form of multiplicity is neither war nor commerce — both presuppose the face and the transcendence of the being appearing in the face.

### War, Violence, and the Structure of the Terms

War presupposes the transcendence of the antagonist — it is waged against *man*. It is surrounded with honors and aims at "a presence that comes always from elsewhere, a being that appears in a face." The possibility the adversary retains of thwarting calculations expresses the separation, the breach of totality.

Violence bears upon "only a being both graspable and escaping every hold." Without this *living contradiction* in the being that undergoes violence, deployment of force would reduce to labor.

**Time as postponement**: "A being independent of and yet at the same time exposed to the other is a temporal being: to the inevitable violence of death it opposes its time, which is postponement itself." Time is not made intelligible by finite freedom — time gives meaning to the notion of finite freedom. Time is the fact that the whole existence of the mortal being is "not being for death, but the 'not yet' which is a way of being against death, a retreat before death in the very midst of its inexorable approach."

**Freedom is not causa sui but deferral**: "Freedom itself is but its adjournment by time." Not finite freedom (a compound of activity and passivity) but "a freedom originally null, offered in death to the other, but in which time arises as a détente" — necessity relaxed and postponed. "Postponement by virtue of which nothing is definitive yet, nothing consummated."

### Corporeity as Ontological Regime

Corporeity is "the mode of existence of a being whose presence is postponed at the very moment of his presence" — a simultaneity of absence and presence, suppleness. This distension within the tension of the instant can only come from an infinite dimension opened by the face of the Other.

The body exceeds the categories of a thing but does not coincide with the role of "lived body." The body in its very activity inverts into a thing to be treated as a thing — "it abides between health and sickness." Through it, one can mistreat the "for itself" of the person — one does not only offend, one *coerces*. ("I am anything you like," says Sganarelle under the blows.) The originality of the body consists in the *coinciding* of two points of view (biological and interior) — this is the paradox and essence of time itself.

### Commerce, Work, and the Historical Relation

The will at work ensures the separated being's being at home with itself. But in its work, the will remains unexpressed — works take on "the anonymity of merchandise." Labor which brings being into our possession ipso facto relinquishes it — "in the very sovereignty of its powers unceremoniously delivered over to the Other."

Every will separates itself from its work. "The movement proper to action consists in issuing in the unknown — in not being able to measure all its consequences." The unknown upon which action issues resists all knowledge because it represents the meaning the work receives from the other. The work is destined to this alien Sinngebung from the moment of its origin.

Power does not accompany its work to the end — a separation opens between producer and product. "His transcendence stops mid-way." Production attests the author in his absence as a plastic form. This inexpressive character is reflected positively in market value — suitability for others, capability to assume meanings others give. The work "does not defend itself against the Other's Sinngebung."

**History is economic history**: History links up in works. There is no purely interior history. In history the will is congealed into a personage interpreted on the basis of his work. Historiography recounts how survivors appropriate dead wills' works — "it rests on the usurpation carried out by the conquerors, that is, by the survivors."

### The Will and Death

The will that escapes itself — that does not contain itself — means others can lay hold of the work: alienating, acquiring, buying, stealing. Commerce aims at the anonymous market; war is waged against a mass. Material things (bread, wine, steel) "have a hold on the 'for itself' of the will." The part of eternal truth in materialism: the human will can be laid hold of in its works.

Even courage does not achieve total independence: the Other may will precisely the death one accepts. "Refusing to serve another by one's life does not preclude serving him by one's death." The being that wills does not exhaust the destiny of his existence in his will.

The will moves between betrayal and fidelity — simultaneous, describing the very originality of its power. Fidelity does not forget betrayal; the religious will remains a relation with the Other. Pardon comes from the outside. "The postponement of death in a mortal will — time — is the mode of existence and reality of a separated being that has entered into relation with the Other."

## Key Concepts for sigma-TAP Integration

1. **Ipseity = existing without genus**: The I is not unique like a unique object but exists without being the individuation of any concept. This is the philosophical ground for modeling agents as irreducible trajectories rather than instances of a type.

2. **Enjoyment accomplishes atheist separation**: L11 identity is not logical opposition but self-sustaining dwelling in the other. The agent's self-relation is enjoyment/happiness, not mere self-reference. Need is a *happy dependence*, not lack.

3. **Consummation vs. consumption**: Life consists in *consummating* nourishments, not merely consuming fuel. Consumption = taking up sedimented structure into new action (process-praxis). Consummation = praxis reaching its term, becoming sedimented (praxis-process). Levinas uses these terms identically to the user's framework.

4. **Time = praxitive deferral**: Time is "postponement itself" — the mortal being's way of being *against* death. Freedom is not causa sui but necessity relaxed and postponed. "Nothing is definitive yet, nothing consummated." Directly maps to the agent persisting as long as it defers consummation.

5. **Corporeity as ontological regime**: The body is the *coinciding* of biological and interior perspectives — simultaneously absence and presence, "postponed at the very moment of presence." This is the structural basis for modeling the agent's exposure to the Other (L12/L21 vulnerability).

6. **Facade vs. face**: The facade is the type_set surface — objects "on exhibition," gleaming but not delivering themselves. The face is the capacity to *exceed* the surface — "its revelation is speech." This grounds the distinction between artifact agents (facade-presenting, surface-determined) and actor agents (face-bearing, capable of transcending their type_set).

7. **Radical multiplicity vs. numerical multiplicity**: Numerical multiplicity is "defenseless against totalization." For genuine pluralism, the relation must proceed "from one unto the other" and remain invisible to a panoramic exterior gaze. This means modeling that preserves individuality must not reduce agents to interchangeable units occupying positions in a totality.

8. **Work separates from will**: The producer-product separation is structural, not accidental. Works take on meanings from foreign wills. "Every will separates itself from its work." This grounds the practico-inert (sedimented past praxis storing potential energy) and the fundamental asymmetry between intention and effect.

9. **The will does not contain itself**: History is constituted by works, not intentions. The will can be "laid hold of" through its products — through gold, steel, bread. Commerce aims at the anonymous market. This is the philosophical basis for L22 dynamics: environmental processes operating through sedimented works independently of their authors' intentions.

10. **Asymmetry of transcendence**: "The I not being transcendent with regard to the other in the same sense that the other is transcendent with regard to me." This is not reciprocal. The Other's transcendence accounts for freedom, not the reverse. This grounds the asymmetric cross-metathesis (initiator ≠ responder).
