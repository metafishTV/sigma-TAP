# Emery & Trist (1965) — The Causal Texture of Organizational Environments

**Source**: Fred Emery & Eric Trist, "The Causal Texture of Organizational Environments," *Human Relations* 18:21–32, 1965. Originally presented at XVII International Congress of Psychology, Washington D.C., August 1963.

**Core contribution**: Introduces the L-matrix notation (L11, L12, L21, L22) for analyzing organizational-environmental interdependencies, and identifies four ideal types of environmental causal texture arranged in increasing complexity.

---

## Theoretical Foundation: Open Systems and Their Limits

The paper begins from systems theory's trajectory. The first phase concerned *internal processes* — relating parts to wholes (organismic biology of Jennings/Cannon/Henderson, Gestalt theory, classical social structure). These could be represented in closed-system models. The second phase related *wholes to environments*, yielding open-system models. Bertalanffy (1950) showed, via the general transport equation, that living entities differ from inanimate objects by importing material from the environment, transforming it according to internal system characteristics, and exporting other types back. This process renders the organism *negentropic* — capable of attaining stability in a time-independent steady state, which is the necessary condition of adaptability.

**Steady state ≠ equilibrium**: Equilibrium states (classical physics, second law of thermodynamics) permit no work when reached. The openness of a steady state *maintains work capacity* — without which adaptability and survival are impossible. Open-system properties include equifinality, growth through internal elaboration, self-regulation, and constancy of direction with change of position — not all yet worked out.

However, Bertalanffy's formulation handles exchange processes between organism/organization and environmental *elements* but does not address processes *in the environment itself* which are among the determining conditions of exchange. This is the gap the paper fills.

## The L-Matrix

The paper's foundational move is thus to argue that open-systems theory handles organism-environment *exchanges* but neglects processes *within the environment itself*. To remedy this, they reintroduce "causal texture" (from Tolman & Brunswik 1935, via Pepper 1934) and propose a 2×2 matrix of potentially lawful connections:

| | Suffix 1 (Organization) | Suffix 2 (Environment) |
|---|---|---|
| **Suffix 1 (Org)** | L11 — internal interdependencies | L12 — org→env transactional |
| **Suffix 2 (Env)** | L21 — env→org transactional | L22 — environmental causal texture |

**Critical point**: The laws governing L22 are often *incommensurate* with those governing L11 or L12/L21. Boundaries are "break points" — one cannot always reduce org-env relations to containment ("being included in"). The javelin analogy: the thrower's sighting-and-throwing actions (L11) are lawfully connected, but the javelin's flight is governed by meteorological systems (L22) that operate under entirely different laws. This incommensurability is not a deficiency but a structural feature of the relationship between organized systems and their environments.

## Four Types of Causal Texture

The types form a series of *increasing causal texturing*. Each step introduces a qualitatively new feature. They exist as "ideal types" — approximations coexisting simultaneously in real environments with varying weightings.

### Type I: Placid, Randomized

- Goals and noxiants ("goods" and "bads") are unchanging in themselves and randomly distributed.
- Corresponds to: Simon's bare surface with scattered food heaps; Ashby's limiting case of no environmental-part connection; Schutzenberger's random field; classical economics' perfect market.
- **Organizational response**: No distinction between tactics and strategy. Optimal strategy = purely local best action. Can only be learned by trial-and-error for particular local variances. Organizations can exist as single, small units.

### Type II: Placid, Clustered

- Goals and noxiants cluster — they are not random but "band together in certain ways."
- Corresponds to: Tolman & Brunswik's concern; Ashby's serial system; economists' imperfect competition.
- Clustering creates *signs* (parts indicate other parts) and *means-objects* (instrumental approach/avoidance).
- **Organizational response**: Strategy emerges as distinct from tactics. Survival becomes critically linked to *knowledge of the environment*. Pursuing nearby goals may lead into danger; avoiding difficulty may lead away from reward. Relevant objective = "optimal location" — some positions discernibly richer than others. Requires: concentration of resources, subordination to main plan, development of "distinctive competence" (Selznick 1957). Tendency toward growth in size, hierarchy, centralized control and coordination.

### Type III: Disturbed-Reactive

- A Type II environment containing *more than one organization of the same kind*. This becomes the dominant feature.
- Corresponds to: Ashby's ultra-stable system; economists' oligopoly.
- Creates *imbrication* (Chein 1943) of causal strands: each organization must consider that its knowledge is also accessible to competitors, that competitors seek the same positions, that competitors know this mutually.
- **Organizational response**: An intermediate level emerges between strategy and tactics — the *operation* (German/Soviet military terminology). Operations = planned campaigns of tactical initiatives + calculated reactions + counteractions. The new element: deciding which of someone else's possible tactics one wishes to occur while preventing others. Requires: decentralization, premium on quality/speed of peripheral decision-making. Objective shifts from optimal location to *capacity/power to move at will* — ability to make and meet competitive challenge. Strategies of absorption and parasitism become relevant. Stability sometimes requires "coming-to-terms" between competitors — knowing when not to fight to the death.

### Type IV: Turbulent Fields

- Dynamic processes creating significant organizational variance arise *from the field itself*. Like Type III (dynamic), unlike Types I–II (static). Unlike Type III, the dynamics arise not just from organizational interaction but from the *ground itself being in motion*.
- **Three contributing trends**:
  1. Organizations grown so large under Type III that their persistent, strong actions induce *autochthonous processes in the environment* (analogy: soldiers marching in step over a bridge — the structure resonates).
  2. Deepening interdependence between economic and other societal facets — economic organizations increasingly enmeshed in legislation and public regulation.
  3. Increasing reliance on R&D for competitive capacity, creating a *continuous change gradient* in the environmental field.
- **Consequence**: Gross increase in *relevant uncertainty*. Action-consequences become increasingly unpredictable; they do not necessarily attenuate with distance but may be amplified unexpectedly at any point. Strongly pursued action-lines may be attenuated by emergent field forces.

## Case Studies

### Case 1: UK Canning Company (Type II → Type IV transition)

A dominant canner (65% market share, stable since prewar) invested in a new automated factory optimized for long production runs. While building, the environment reorganized through L22 processes invisible to the company:

1. Small canners appeared (imported fruits) because postwar controls on steel/tin were lifted → cheap cans available.
2. These canners needed winter work → quick-frozen food development created surplus crops unsuitable for freezing but suitable for canning → imported cheaply (initially from US, then underdeveloped countries).
3. Society's increasing affluence → more consumers could afford quick-frozen (premium) form → company's canned product lost its premier position.
4. Simultaneously, supermarkets and grocery chains developed → wanted house-brand products → bulk orders to small canners at very low cost (no marketing expenses, cheaper raw materials) → captured >50% of market within three years (retailer varieties had been <1%).

The automated factory couldn't adapt. Traditional management failed to see that *independently arising events were becoming connected to each other* in ways leading to irreversible general change. Result: board split, group headquarters intervention, management upheaval, painful redefinition of mission, altered product mix, new identity. This demonstrates L22 dynamics — the environment's own causal texture reorganized in ways not reducible to any single L12 or L21 relationship.

### Case 2: National Farmers Union (full Type IV)

NFU (200,000+ of 250,000 English/Welsh farmers). Presenting problem: communications breakdown (HQ↔county↔local branches). Actual problem: environmental turbulence.

Historical stability: government price-support via annual reviews (wartime/postwar food security imperative). This created NFU cohesion. As thermonuclear stalemate made wartime food security logic obsolete, support declined differentially — small/inefficient farmers exposed, while large mechanized farms thrived.

Simultaneously: (a) supply side consolidated into few giant firms (fertilizer, machinery, seeds, veterinary) exerting power over farmers; (b) marketing side transformed by organized food-processing/distributing industries, dominant firms dictating contract terms. Farmer autonomy eroded from both sides.

Key finding: the "communications problem" was actually the organization interpreting environmental change signals through its traditional frame. Only parallel analysis of environment AND organization enabled progress. Challenges included: creating a technical bureaucratic elite capable of long-range planning while preserving democratic values; developing mediating institutions between agriculture and other economic sectors without triggering massive competition. Britain's possible Common Market entry further complicated everything.

The solution required farmers to cede decision-making power (what to produce, how, to what standard, how to market) to NFU — anathema to intensely individualistic farmers. They were being asked to "redefine identity, reverse basic values, and refashion organization — all at the same time." Progress remains fitful, slow, conflict-ridden.

## Values as Coping Mechanism for Type IV

Under Type IV conditions, individual organizations *cannot adapt successfully through direct action alone*. The paper proposes that the adaptive response analogous to strategy (Type II) and operations (Type III) is the *emergence of values with overriding significance for all field members*.

Social values = coping mechanisms for persisting areas of relevant uncertainty. When action-consequences become untraceable through extended social fields, categorical rules (like the Ten Commandments) provide guidance and ready calculus. Values are neither strategies nor tactics — they have the character of *power fields* (Lewin 1936) and act as *injunctions*.

**Transformative effect**: When effective values emerge, richly joined turbulent fields are *transformed* — simplified and made relatively static. The relevance of events is given directly in the ethical code rather than sought through intricate diverging causal strands. This transformation is regressive or constructively adaptive depending on whether emergent values adequately represent new environmental requirements.

Against Ashby's pessimism (richly connected large environments may exceed human adaptive capacity, the brain being an ultra-stable system), Emery & Trist argue that such environments may be not only adaptable-to but increasingly characteristic of the human condition since settled communities began. Values can be rational, and their rationality strengthens as scientific ethos takes hold.

## Matrix Organization

Turbulent fields demand organizational forms essentially different from hierarchies:
- Type III requires accommodation between *like but competitive* organizations (negatively correlated fates).
- Type IV requires relationships between *dissimilar* organizations whose fates are *positively correlated* — maximizing cooperation, recognizing no single organization can become paramount.

This is called an **organizational matrix**: delimits on value criteria what/who may be included in the field, enabling shape without formal hierarchy. Professional associations are one experienced model.

**Institutionalization** (Selznick 1957): organizations become institutions through embodying values relating them to wider society. The strategic objective shifts from location (Type II) or power (Type III) to *institutional success*. Strategic planning requires not only goal-congruence with organizational character but selection of goal-paths offering *maximum convergence of interests* with other parties.

Organizations arising to meet Type IV problems (e.g., NEDC) risk being misconstrued in Type III terms, with attempts to secure monolithic power — resisted overtly in democracies, covertly elsewhere. Matrix organization implies McGregor's Theory Y, which implies new values. Values emerge slowly — Likert estimates 10–15 years for a large organization, at minimum a generation for a whole society. Whether this is fast enough given accelerating Type IV salience is an open and urgent question.

## Key Concepts for sigma-TAP Integration

1. **L-matrix as foundational schema**: L11 (internal), L12 (org→env), L21 (env→org), L22 (env↔env causal texture). The paper establishes L22 as the analytically neglected but decisive domain.

2. **Incommensurability**: L22 laws ≠ L11 laws. This is structural, not accidental. Relevant for modeling: environmental dynamics should not be simple extensions of agent dynamics.

3. **Type progression as increasing organization-environment coupling**: From isolated local optimization (I) through strategic positioning (II) and competitive operations (III) to field-level turbulence where the ground moves (IV).

4. **Autochthonous processes**: In Type IV, large organizations' actions induce self-sustaining dynamics in the environment — the environment develops its own agency. Relevant for modeling emergent environmental behavior in sigma-TAP.

5. **Values as field-simplification**: Under extreme uncertainty, shared values transform turbulent fields into tractable ones. The value-system is not an ornament but a structural coping mechanism — a field-level attractor that reduces relevant uncertainty.

6. **Irreversibility**: Environmental reorganization (L22) can produce irreversible change invisible to organizations focused on L11/L12. The canning company's failure was not operational but perceptual — they couldn't see L22 connections forming.

7. **Matrix ≠ hierarchy**: Type IV responses require non-hierarchical coordination among dissimilar entities with positively correlated fates. Absorption/parasitism (Type III strategies) are insufficient.

8. **Temporal asymmetry**: Environmental change can outpace organizational adaptation. Values emerge slowly (generation-scale). This creates a structural lag that is itself a source of turbulence.
