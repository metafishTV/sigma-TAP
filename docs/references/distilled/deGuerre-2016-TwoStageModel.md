# de Guerre (2016) — Open Systems Theory and the Two-Stage Model of Active Adaptation

**Source**: Donald W. de Guerre, "Open Systems Theory and the Two-Stage Model of Active Adaptation," chapter in practitioner anthology, 2016. Published via stsroundtable.com.

**Core contribution**: Practitioner-oriented exposition of the complete two-stage model of active adaptive planning (Search Conference + Participative Design Workshop). Contains the "Tele and Tinny" society diagram (Figure 3.1) showing DP1's cascading pathological spiral, the two-stage model diagram (Figure 3.2), the SC schematic framework (Figure 3.3), and the DP1/DP2 structural diagrams (Figure 3.4). Includes a detailed case study of a 300-employee open-pit mine redesign that demonstrates the full two-stage process from Search Conferences through Participative Design Workshops to organizational consensus.

---

## 1. Socioecology and the Unit of Analysis

The chapter opens by establishing the fundamental unit of analysis: "people in environment" -- socioecology. The system cannot be studied in isolation from its environment, nor the environment without its embedded systems. Every organizational analysis must take the form "system-in-environment." This is not merely a methodological preference but an ontological claim: the system IS a system-in-environment. Removing it from context changes what it is.

This framing positions OST against traditions that analyze either the system (classical management, bureaucratic theory) or the environment (market theory, ecological models) in isolation. The two-stage model operationalizes this by requiring that environmental analysis PRECEDE AND INFORM organizational redesign.

## 2. The Tele and Tinny Society (Figure 3.1)

The "Tele and Tinny" diagram is de Guerre's most conceptually significant contribution. It maps the cascading pathology of DP1 (bureaucratic) organization through time, showing an irreversible self-amplifying spiral:

**The causal chain**:

1. **DP1 structure** (redundancy of parts, responsibility located one level above work) produces a specific emotional environment.
2. **Negative emotional tone** emerges: anxiety, frustration, resentment, helplessness. Workers experience the six criteria as degraded -- insufficient autonomy, blocked learning, monotony, absence of mutual support, meaninglessness, no desirable future.
3. **Defense mechanisms** activate: cliques form (not around task interdependencies but around pre-existing trust relationships), competition intensifies between units and individuals, withdrawal (psychological or physical) occurs. These are not individual pathologies but structural responses to structural conditions.
4. **Productivity loss** follows from defense mechanisms: information hoarding, blame displacement, passive resistance, absenteeism, turnover. The organization produces less value per unit of human energy invested.
5. **Societal harm** radiates outward: the pathological patterns learned at work are carried into families, communities, civic life. DP1 organizations export their dysfunction into the wider social field.
6. **Self-amplifying spiral**: Societal harm reinforces the conditions that make DP1 seem necessary (distrust, low capability, need for control), which reinforces DP1 structure, which produces more negative emotional tone, and the cycle deepens.

**Critical feature**: The time axis. The Tele and Tinny diagram explicitly shows this as a process OVER TIME, not a static state. The spiral is irreversible under its own dynamics -- DP1 does not self-correct. Each turn of the spiral produces conditions that make the next turn more likely and more severe. The only interruption comes from external structural intervention (the PDW).

**The naming**: "Tele and Tinny" refers to television and tinned food -- the consumer society that DP1 organizations produce. Workers deskilled and exhausted by DP1 work become passive consumers rather than active citizens. The socioecological unit (people-in-environment) degrades at both poles simultaneously.

## 3. The Two-Stage Model (Figure 3.2)

The two-stage model is the complete active adaptive planning methodology:

**Stage 1: Search Conference (SC)**
- Purpose: Examine the system-in-environment relationship over time
- Function: Elicits ideal-seeking behavior; creates alignment between organizations and their environments
- Output: Strategic direction, action plans, shared understanding of environmental demands

**Stage 2: Participative Design Workshop (PDW)**
- Purpose: Align people and work within the organization
- Function: Makes the organization agile, resilient, and healthy by restructuring from DP1 to DP2
- Output: Self-managing team designs, skills matrices, goal agreements, implementation plans

**Combined effect**: Active adaptive planning. The two stages address the two fundamental relationships:
- SC addresses L11 <-> L22 (system-environment transaction)
- PDW addresses intra-L11 (internal system structure)

The sequence is essential: environment first (SC), then structure (PDW). Reversing the sequence -- restructuring before understanding the environment -- produces organizations that are internally coherent but maladapted. The two-stage model prevents this by ensuring environmental realities constrain structural choices.

## 4. Search Conference: The Community-Building Event

The Search Conference is explicitly NOT a small-group technique. It is a community-building event that uses large-group process design. Key characteristics:

### The Funnel Analogy

The SC process follows a funnel structure -- broad environmental scanning progressively narrowing to specific action plans:

1. **Wide environmental scan** (L22-dominant): What is happening in the world? Global trends, technological shifts, social changes, ecological forces. Past, present, and probable futures. This is deliberately broad -- participants are mapping the causal texture of the extended social field.

2. **Narrowing to the econiche** (L12/L21 transition): From the broad environment, what specifically affects us? Which environmental forces impinge on our system? This is the task environment -- the immediate transactional field.

3. **System history and identity** (L11): What have we been? Key events, turning points, achievements, failures, persistent patterns. This grounds the community in its own trajectory.

4. **Desirable and probable futures** (Integration): Given what we know about the environment AND about ourselves, what futures are both desirable and achievable? This is the integration phase -- synthesizing L22 awareness with L11 knowledge.

5. **Constraints and achievable future**: What prevents us from reaching the desirable future? What is realistically achievable given constraints?

6. **Action plans**: Specific, concrete plans with timelines, responsibilities, and resource requirements. The funnel's narrow end.

### SC Schematic Framework (Figure 3.3)

The schematic framework maps the SC process onto the OST theoretical architecture:

| SC Phase | L-Channel | OST Concept |
|---|---|---|
| Environment scanning | L22 | Extended Social Field |
| Desirable/Probable Future Environment | L22 | Environmental causal texture |
| Econiche analysis | L12/L21 | Task Environment |
| Desirable/Probable Econiche | L12/L21 | Transactional field |
| System history | L11 | System principle |
| Desirable Future | L11/Integration | Ideal-seeking |
| Constraints | All channels | Directive correlation assessment |
| Achievable Future | Integration | Active adaptation target |
| Action Plans | L12 | Planning function |
| System grows and diffuses | All channels | Active adaptation realized |

### Essential SC Characteristics

- **Integration includes rationalizing conflict**: The SC does not seek premature consensus. Conflicts are surfaced and rationalized -- their structural sources identified and addressed. This is distinct from suppression (DP1) or avoidance (laissez-faire).

- **Diffusion as essential characteristic**: The SC's outputs are not confined to participants. The event is designed so that results diffuse outward -- participants become carriers of the shared environmental understanding and strategic direction. Without diffusion, the SC remains an isolated event rather than a systemic intervention.

- **Community, not committee**: The SC creates community (shared purpose, mutual engagement, collective ownership) rather than committee (delegated authority, representative structure, DP1 logic). This distinction is critical -- committees are DP1 structures that reproduce the pathologies they purport to solve.

## 5. Participative Design Workshop: Structural Transformation

### Two Forms of PDW

1. **Redesign of existing DP1 structure**: The more common form. Workers analyze their current DP1 organization, diagnose its Six Criteria deficiencies, and design a DP2 replacement.

2. **Design from scratch**: For new operations, greenfield sites, or organizations willing to start fresh. Workers design DP2 structure without the constraint of existing DP1 patterns.

### Three Phases

**Phase 1 -- Analysis**: Briefing on the six criteria and DP1 design principle. Workers (not consultants, not management) analyze their current work against each criterion. This phase produces the diagnostic -- a shared, worker-generated assessment of how badly DP1 is failing them.

**Phase 2 -- Change**: Briefing on DP2 design principle. Workers redesign their own work structure: defining self-managing teams, mapping task interdependencies, creating skills matrices, establishing coordination mechanisms. The redesign must satisfy all six criteria.

**Phase 3 -- Practicalities**: Goals, training needs, career paths, industrial relations, implementation timeline. This phase bridges design and reality -- addressing the practical requirements for transitioning from the current DP1 structure to the proposed DP2 structure.

### The Six Criteria as Intrinsic Motivators

De Guerre presents the six criteria as intrinsic motivators for productive work (from Fred Emery):

1. **Adequate elbow room for decision-making** -- autonomy within one's scope
2. **Opportunity for learning on an ongoing basis** -- continuous growth, not just initial training
3. **An optimal level of variety** -- enough diversity to maintain engagement
4. **Mutual support and respect** -- the group cares for its members
5. **Meaningfulness** -- the work contributes to something valued
6. **A desirable future** -- current work opens pathways to valued future states

### Two Genotypical Design Principles

**DP1 (Redundancy of Parts)**: Each person is a replaceable part, skilled in one narrow function. Coordination responsibility is located one level above. The structure is hierarchical: Manager -> Supervisor -> People/Tasks. Goals are set from above and cascaded down. If a person fails, they are replaced. The system is designed around the assumption that parts will fail and must be swappable.

**DP2 (Redundancy of Functions)**: Each person carries multiple skills (redundant functions). Coordination responsibility is located with the people doing the work. The structure is a self-managing group: Management Team provides boundary management, Team Goals are negotiated, People + Whole Task form a circle of shared responsibility. Goals are set by negotiation between the team and management. If a person is absent, others can cover because they share skills.

### DP1 vs DP2 Structural Diagrams (Figure 3.4)

**DP1 diagram**: Hierarchical tree. Manager at top, Supervisor in middle, People and Tasks at bottom. Arrows flow downward (command) and upward (reporting). Goals set from above. Coordination is external to the work group.

**DP2 diagram**: Circular arrangement. Management Team at boundary. People and Whole Task in a circle. Team Goals negotiated between the circle and the Management Team. Goals by negotiation. Coordination is internal to the work group. The "whole task" is critical -- not fragmented subtasks but the complete meaningful unit of work.

## 6. Case Study: 300-Employee Open-Pit Mine Redesign

This case study demonstrates the full two-stage model in operation.

### Context
- 300 employees in an open-pit mining operation
- Organization structured along DP1 lines (hierarchical, specialized, supervisor-dependent)
- Need for transformation identified through prior strategic work

### Stage 1: Search Conferences
- **4 Search Conferences** conducted, each involving approximately 35 employees
- Employees drawn from across the organization -- not selected by management hierarchy but by representation of the full workforce
- SC process followed the standard funnel: environmental scanning -> econiche -> system history -> desirable futures -> action plans
- Output: **8 desirable future themes** emerged across the 4 SCs -- convergent strategic direction generated from distributed community process

### Stage 2: Participative Design Workshops
- **11 PDWs** conducted following the SCs
- Each PDW involved workers from specific operational areas
- Workers analyzed their current DP1 structure against the six criteria
- Workers designed their own DP2 alternatives
- Output: **44 proposed designs** -- each PDW group producing multiple design options

### Integration and Selection
- The 44 proposed designs were analyzed for common patterns
- **3 basic design themes** emerged from the 44 proposals
- **1 design theme was rejected** because it did not meet DP2 specifications -- it retained DP1 elements (coordination located above the work group rather than with it). This rejection demonstrates that the DP2 specification functions as a genuine constraint, not a preference. Designs that look participative but retain DP1 control structures are identified and excluded.
- **Town hall meeting**: 27 out of 31 groups chose Design A through a participative voting process. This was not unanimous but was a clear consensus -- the remaining 4 groups were accommodated within the Design A framework with local variations.

### Character of the Process

De Guerre's assessment of the process is revealing:

"Not an intellectual engineering exercise; it was very much a political and emotional process."

The redesign was not a rational-analytic optimization. It involved conflict, negotiation, anxiety, loss, identity threat, and creative emergence. Workers were not merely solving a design problem -- they were restructuring their relationships, their identities, their daily experience. This is why the process cannot be done by consultants or management: only the people whose lives are affected can navigate the political and emotional dimensions.

"Painful learning."

The learning that occurs through the PDW process is not comfortable. Workers confront the pathologies of DP1 that they have internalized -- the dependency, the competition, the narrow identity. They must unlearn DP1 habits while simultaneously learning DP2 practices. This is structurally painful, not accidentally so.

### Outcomes
- Generative learning occurred at three levels: **individual** (workers developed new skills and broadened their identities), **team** (groups learned to self-manage, coordinate, and support), and **organizational** (the entire 300-person operation learned to function as an integrated DP2 system)
- The case demonstrates that the two-stage model scales: 4 SCs feeding into 11 PDWs feeding into a single organizational consensus, involving 300 people in a coherent participative process

## 7. Organizational Choice

De Guerre's concluding argument:

"Organizations are perfectly designed to deliver what they do."

This is not praise but diagnosis. If an organization produces pathology (deskilling, competition, passive consumers, societal harm), that is what its design produces. DP1 organizations are perfectly designed to produce the Tele and Tinny spiral. The pathology is not an accident, a failure of implementation, or a character flaw of workers -- it is the structural output of DP1 design.

The corollary: DP2 organizations are perfectly designed to produce learning, mutual support, intrinsic motivation, and active citizenship. The outcomes are structural, not motivational. You cannot motivate people out of DP1 pathology; you must redesign the structure.

De Guerre argues that DP2 has been proven more effective than both autocracy (DP1 in its pure form) and representative democracy (which retains DP1 through delegation). DP2 is participative democracy -- responsibility located with the people affected by decisions, not with representatives who stand in for them. This is not an ideological claim but an empirical one, grounded in decades of case studies across industries and cultures.

## Key Concepts for sigma-TAP Integration

### 1. Tele and Tinny Spiral = Demetathesis Cascade

The Tele and Tinny diagram maps directly to demetathesis -- the self-amplifying pathological spiral that occurs when DP1 structure is imposed or when DP2 structure collapses. The causal chain (DP1 -> negative affect -> defense mechanisms -> productivity loss -> societal harm -> reinforced DP1) is a structural cascade, not an oscillation. It maps to temporal state regression in sigma-TAP: established (state 4) -> desituated (state 3) -> inertial (state 1) -> disintegrated (state 0). Each step produces conditions that make recovery less likely. The time axis is critical -- this is irreversible under its own dynamics. Only external structural intervention (the PDW, which is itself a metathetic event) can break the spiral.

### 2. SC Funnel = L22 -> L21 -> L11 -> L12 Scanning Sequence

The Search Conference's funnel process maps to a systematic scan across L-channels in a specific order: begin with L22 (broad environmental causal texture), narrow to L21 (environment-to-system impacts on the econiche), then examine L11 (system history and identity), and finally project through L12 (action plans, system-to-environment interventions). This sequence -- environment first, then system, then action -- is exactly the user's "topology from sigma" sequencing: the environmental feedback signal (sigma) must be read before organizational structure (topology) can be meaningfully designed. The SC makes this sequence explicit and methodological.

### 3. Emergence Through Variation and Selection

The case study's numbers are instructive: 4 SCs -> 8 themes -> 11 PDWs -> 44 proposed designs -> 3 basic themes -> 1 rejected -> Design A selected by 27/31 groups. This is emergence through variation (many designs generated from distributed process) and selection (designs converge on structural attractors, non-DP2 designs rejected, consensus emerges). The winning design was not engineered from above but emerged from below through a participative process. This maps to the user's principle that "actors and artifacts emerge from the making, not hard-coded as made." In sigma-TAP: group topology should emerge from agent interaction history (many possible configurations explored, structural attractors selected through practice), not from imposed initial conditions.

### 4. Town Hall Consensus = Group-Level Phase Alignment

The town hall meeting where 27/31 groups chose Design A represents a moment of group-level phase alignment. This is not unanimity but sufficient convergence -- enough agents sharing compatible understandings (TAPS signatures) and sufficient trust (tau_group) to collectively select a topology. In sigma-TAP: group-level decisions (topology formation, design principle selection) should require a threshold proportion of agents with compatible signatures and trust levels. The 27/31 ratio (~87%) suggests that near-unanimity is achievable through participative process but absolute unanimity is not required.

### 5. Irreversibility of the DP1 Spiral

The Tele and Tinny diagram's time axis and irreversibility map to the user's concept of temporal state as irreversible drift under DP1 conditions. Demetathesis is not oscillatory -- it is a one-way spiral unless structurally interrupted. This has modeling implications: once a group in sigma-TAP begins the demetathetic cascade (defense mechanisms, withdrawal, type_set atrophy), the cascade should accelerate, not self-correct. Recovery requires a structural intervention equivalent to the PDW -- a metathetic event that restructures the group's coordination principle from DP1 to DP2. Without such intervention, the group progresses toward disintegration.

### 6. Political and Emotional Process

De Guerre's insistence that the redesign was "not an intellectual engineering exercise" but "a political and emotional process" involving "painful learning" has modeling implications. Metathetic events in sigma-TAP should not be frictionless rational optimizations. They should involve cost (the "painful" dimension), conflict (the "political" dimension), and identity transformation (the "emotional" dimension). The current sigma-TAP model captures cost through dormancy periods and temporal state hysteresis, but the political and emotional dimensions -- conflict between agents with different design preferences, identity threat when type_sets are restructured -- are future modeling targets.

### 7. Three Levels of Generative Learning

The case study identifies learning at individual, team, and organizational levels. In sigma-TAP: metathetic events should produce learning (type_set expansion, trust accumulation, signature evolution) at three corresponding scales: per-agent (L11 self-metathesis), per-group (L12/L21 reciprocal cross-metathesis within family groups), and system-wide (L22 environmental transitions that shift the entire field). The three levels are not independent -- individual learning feeds group learning, which feeds organizational learning, which feeds back to individual opportunity. This is the multi-scale coupling that the sigma feedback function must capture.
