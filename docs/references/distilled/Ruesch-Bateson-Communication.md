# Ruesch & Bateson (1951) — Communication: The Social Matrix of Psychiatry

**Source**: Jurgen Ruesch & Gregory Bateson, *Communication: The Social Matrix of Psychiatry* (New York: Norton, 1951). Excerpted: Chapter 7 (pp. 186–211), Chapter 8 (pp. 212–227), and Conclusion (pp. 273–299).

**Core contribution**: Establishes a four-level architecture of communication networks (intrapersonal, interpersonal, group, cultural) with distinct structural properties at each level. Introduces metacommunication, deutero-learning, and the epistemological limits of self-corrective systems. Provides the conceptual architecture that Emery & Trist's L-matrix formalizes.

## Chapter 7: Information and Codification

### The Minimal System and Eight Types of Codification-Evaluation

R&B begin from a non-human model: a minimal causal circuit — an entity with an internal self-corrective loop that acts upon and is acted upon by an environment (a protozoan engaged in heliotropism, or a servomechanism seeking a target). This system is incapable of complex codification. From this carte blanche, they systematically anthropomorphize upward through eight types of codification-evaluation:

1. **Discrimination of environmental entities**: Recognition and classification of environmental Gestalten ("oak trees," "light"). These are arbitrary but interdependent — having discriminated "oak trees" from "light," the discrimination of "elms" from "oaks" likely follows. Relatively trivial for communication because misunderstandings at this level are easily resolved through pointing at objects.

2. **Subdivision of the organism**: Recognition of body parts, sensations, actions. Enables association of sensations with body parts and conceptualization of purposes in terms of localized sensation — already a narrowing of the totality of organism-environment interaction. Also makes possible falsification of parts of the body image and projection onto other circuit parts.

3. **Self/environment distinction**: Drawing a closed line anywhere on the circuit diagram and regarding everything inside as "self," everything outside as "environment." This is arbitrary — the organism may include external objects within the self while labeling body parts as environment. There is no right way of delimiting the self. Conflicting premises about self-boundaries lead to communication failure, frustration, and ultimately hostility and pathology.

4. **Conceptualization of control**: A step beyond mere differentiation — enables the organism to perceive the environment as coercive or itself as coercing the environment. Both are usually false simplifications of the reality of interaction. Diversities include all attributions of passivity and activity to self and environment.

5. **Internal causal arcs within the self**: Premises like "I am the captain of my soul" and the mind-body dichotomy. The supposed internal splitting of the individual is likely a symbolic echo of presumed relations between self and environment, or vice versa — splits within the self expressed as premises about self-environment relationship.

6. **Multiple levels of abstraction**: The organism perceives Gestalt A and Gestalt B and codifies on the assumption that there is a relationship (likeness or unlikeness) between them. This relationship is *more abstract* than either Gestalt. Through such steps the codification system becomes elaborately multi-leveled, with interrelations capable of great diversity. Analogical reasoning enters here — using premises from one domain to codify another, which may lead to error but need not.

7. **Gestalten involving time spans**: The organism may see a single movement as an "act" or whole sequences (including actions and their results) as having unity, purpose, or failure. May conceptualize the limitations of its own life and symbolically extend these — seeing an initiation ceremony or therapeutic experience as "death and rebirth."

8. **Reification of concepts**: The organism turns upon its own codification system. When sufficient complexity permits two or more levels of abstraction, the organism can treat higher abstractions as though equivalent to lower ones — endowing abstract concepts with causal or controlling efficacy. "Morality" (an abstraction from actions/words) may be seen as "binding" upon actions. The organism may respect or revolt against "cultural conventions" or even sneer at "death."

### Three Types of Internal Contradiction

Every Gestalt label is an arbitrary condensation of unsummarized data. Multiple overlapping labels are simultaneously applied. Inevitably some will have contrary value or implications:

**(a) Overlapping Gestalten at the same level of abstraction**: A and B are two Gestalt unities with items in common. If A is positively valued and B negatively valued, items in the overlap are positively valued when perceived as part of A, negatively when perceived as part of B. Critically, perception of A does not necessarily promote perception of B — the tendency is mutual *hindering*. This is not oscillation but stable ambivalence.

**(b) Russellian paradox / oscillation**: The class of classes which are not members of themselves — is it a member of itself? If yes, then no; if no, then yes. Mechanical model: an electric buzzer where current through the armature activates an electromagnet that breaks the circuit, but breaking the circuit deactivates the magnet, restoring current. The "yes" and "no" are each applied at two levels of abstraction simultaneously. The phenomenon is not static indecision but *temporal oscillation* — temporary acceptance of one pole promotes preference for the other. Increasing familiarity with a Gestalt leads to its rejection in favor of another, which itself later becomes unacceptable. The attempt to resolve the conflict in favor of one polarity *ipso facto generates preference for the contrasting pole*. This mechanism is profoundly different from (a).

**(c) Circular preference**: When possibilities are offered in pairs, A may be preferred to B, B to C, and C to A. When all three are simultaneously present, decision becomes impossible. Possible mechanisms: (i) evaluation of each member is a function of the other's presence in the pair, (ii) the decision mechanism consists of multiple linked sub-entities each with its own preference (like a voting population — with three equal parties, the swing voter can produce circular outcomes), (iii) McCulloch's neurological circuits and Von Neumann game solutions.

Whether all internal contradictions reduce to these three types is unknown.

### One-Way Communication: The Unobserved Observer

**Major generalization**: The correction of mistakes is the basic means of communication and is actually the only sort of communication which will permit an unobserved observer to form inferences about the codification-evaluation system of the observed.

If an observer sees an organism moving straight toward light, he cannot even recognize a tropism. Only by seeing repeated *self-corrections* when the course deviates can he learn anything. Experiment and observation of self-correction yield the same type of data. The observer learns the polarities of the codification system — what discriminations the organism can make, upon what cues it acts, whether it perceives characteristics of its own actions.

Even when the observed organism talks to itself: (a) if the observer doesn't know the language, his only way to understanding is treating verbal behavior and action sequences as a single system of self-correction — each word gaining meaning by its role in the correction process; (b) if the observer knows the language, he either learns nothing new (projecting his own understanding) or discovers his own mistakes in interpretation, putting him back in position (a). In all cases, meaning depends on self-corrective action.

### Intrapersonal Communication: The Self-Observer

What are the limits of self-observation? Three problems: (a) describing self-observation without personifying a self within the self; (b) distinguishing "new" understanding from working out pre-existing contradictions; (c) the formal limits arising from the fact that an individual can perceive his own life and actions *only in terms of his own system of codification-evaluation* — he is unable to perceive the characteristics of the system in terms of which he perceives.

**Critical finding**: An organism whose premises are true and sufficient for its environment (always achieving goals, never frustrated) can achieve *no new insight* into its own processes and indeed no learning of any kind. This is the abstract "player" in a Von Neumann game. Learning requires error — being "put in the wrong" by the environment.

When the organism is put in error by the environment, it corrects itself — not merely modifying action but modifying the basic processes and mechanisms by which actions are related to cues. It has modified its *system of self-correction*. But this does not necessarily yield *insight into the self*. The organism perceives itself through the new premises as it previously did through the old, but need not see itself as an entity which has characteristically accomplished this change. For such comparative self-perception, it would need to operate as two persons between whom contrast can be stated — doing something that normally occurs in a two-person system.

A **two-person system will always be necessary for insight therapy**, though not for other types of learning. (Other learning may happen to occur in two-person contexts but doesn't require the second person.)

### Two-Person Communication and Metacommunication

Between two human beings there can never be a total lack of mutual understanding — even misunderstanding requires shared premises (about being alive, capable of communication, about anger and pain). The beginnings of a common codification system are latent in biological nature — common anatomy, bodily functioning, maturation.

**Qualitatively new features of two-person systems**:

1. **Messages of a different order of complexity**: Interpersonal messages reach the level of Gestalten because words and postures already refer to complex synthesized units. Interpersonal communication is "pathetically impoverished compared with the richness of intrapersonal consciousness, which in its turn is but an impoverished and restricted version of the total psychic life." But interpersonal messages are already-elaborated products from another's psychic life — profoundly different from perception of inanimate environment. The perceiving individual has *less freedom* because the matter is already synthesized into Gestalten by the communicator.

2. **Bodily processes as analogue supplement**: Each person can enrich verbal streams with simultaneous observation of the other's bodily processes (posture, tension, flushing), intelligible through common biological background and cultural conditioning.

3. **The group as determinant**: The condition for a determinative group is mutual awareness of perception — "if I know that the other perceives me and he knows that I perceive him." This mutual awareness becomes a part-determinant of all action and interaction. This is *qualitatively different* from mutual irritability or responsiveness (as in sticklebacks' reproductive behavior, where there is complex mutual responsiveness but no evidence of awareness of the other's perception). This evolutionary step probably occurred first among mammals, perhaps only among primates and animals domesticated by humans.

4. **Metacommunication**: Defined as "communication about communication" — all exchanged cues and propositions about (a) codification and (b) relationship between communicators. These two subcategories overlap — a very small shift in emphasis can cause a proposition to appear to shift between them because: (a) statements about relationship must still be codified, and (b) every statement in a given codification implicitly affirms that codification. The mere act of communicating carries "we are communicating" — often the most important message sent.

**Operational criteria for mutual awareness** (determining a "higher order" group): (a) signals whose only meaning is acknowledgment of another's signal; (b) signals requesting repetition; (c) signals indicating failure to receive; (d) punctuation of signal streams; (e) ceasing repetition after acknowledgment; (f) deliberate falsehood (requires awareness of other's perception to motivate and succeed).

**Similarity and conflict**: Two persons may be similar or dissimilar in their codification characteristics. Similar persons may conflict precisely at points of resemblance (both saying "it's mine" — phrasings superficially alike but discrepant because the selves don't coincide). Different persons may cooperate in complementary, asymmetrical relationship. It is not necessary for cooperating partners to *understand* each other's universe, though recognition of difference may be important.

## Chapter 8: Conventions of Communication — Where Validity Depends upon Belief

### Deutero-Learning

Starting from Hull's demonstration that a subject learning to recite nonsense syllables not only learns the given series but becomes *more skilled in learning nonsense syllables* — learning a second series faster than the first, a third faster than the second, approaching an asymptotic limit. This is **deutero-learning**: learning to learn.

**The hypothesis** (not yet experimentally verified at time of writing but supported by Harlow's "learning sets"): If deutero-learning occurs in rote contexts, it likely occurs in *all* types of learning contexts. The experimental subject with repeated experience in instrumental contexts becomes skilled at dealing with instrumental contexts generally. This creates a characteristic orientation to the world:

- **Instrumental subject**: Expects a world of contexts amenable to instrumental response; threshold for recognizing such contexts is lowered. Meets the world instrumentally, seeks and responds to appropriately structured contexts, *thereby reinforcing the belief that the world is instrumental*.
- **Pavlovian subject**: Expects a world where he has no control over good and evil; looks for omens of disaster but does not conceive of acting to prevent it. Acts fatally, *thereby reinforcing fatalistic premises*.
- **Avoidance subject**: Oriented toward avoiding punishment rather than seeking positive gain.

**Character structure** emerges from the *context of learning, not the content*. The psychiatrist isn't concerned with whether the patient can typewrite but with the context in which typing was learned — if in a context of threatened punishment, that illuminates character structure.

### Propositions Whose Validity Depends on Belief

This is the chapter's central concept. The instrumental subject inhabits a world where his propositions about instrumentality are apparently verified — being instrumental, he meets the world instrumentally, seeks out instrumental contexts, and reinforces his belief. The propositions are not true or false in a simple objective sense; they are *more true if we believe and act upon them, more false if we disbelieve*. Their validity is a function of belief.

The paranoid, by acting on distrust, creates relationships that reinforce paranoid premises — finding people remarkably untrustworthy. This applies to a "whole host of aberrational premises."

### Relationship Paradigms: The Dominance-Dependence Ambiguity

Using the formalism a'ba" (where a' is A's initiating action, b is B's response, a" is A's completion), R&B show that paradigms for seemingly different relationships can be formally identical. "A instrumentally dominates B": a' = command, b = obedience, a" = reinforcement. "A is dependent on B": a' = signal of need, b = helping, a" = acceptance. Both have the form a'ba".

The participants have a "curious freedom to impose their own interpretations upon the sequences of interaction." A plea for help or a command? A helping act or obedience? A condescending reward or a dependent's acknowledgment? A and B *need not agree in their perceptions at this level*. The Jeeves/Wooster illustration: Bertie is free to see himself as master; Jeeves is free to see himself as succoring Bertie.

**The definition of a relationship depends not merely on the skeleton of events but on how individuals see and interpret those events** — interpretation guided by deutero-learned character-structure premises, further enabled by selective awareness and by the individual's own contribution to creating appropriate action sequences.

### Cultural Premises and the Circular Propagation of Values

Cultural premises (family constellation, role, status, class, caste) and cultural values (morality, success, change, equality, sociability) are all propositions whose validity depends on belief. Value propositions are metacommunicative — their validity depends on the occurrence of concrete actions and words that result from people's acceptance of the values. The mechanism of propagation is circular.

This extends to international relations: the gradual breakdown of modus vivendi is slowly documented at a metacommunicative level, leading to "bitter agreement upon the use of force" — which has the same abstractness and unreality/reality characteristic of all belief-dependent truths. "If, of two nations, each comes to believe in the hostility of the other, that hostility is real... but unreal — and there is therefore always some hope — insofar as the belief is conceivably reversible."

### Play, Art, Religion

**Play**: Contains "I am lying" openly — rules are set up as fictions, competition as fluctuating fiction, codification of gain/loss as fictional devices. "It's only a game."

**Art vs. propaganda**: The propagandist insists his metacommunicative message is an objective statement. The artist says honestly "This is my creation" or "This is how I react to my world" — containing the potentialities for paradox present in "I am lying." Art honestly combines the metacommunicative with the objective. This is "perhaps the greatest formal distinction between art and propaganda."

**Religion**: The great storehouse of deutero-learned propositions summarized in words like "fatalism," "instrumentalism," "free will," "determinism," "responsibility," "guilt," "acceptance of the universe or revolt against it." Religious statements carry implicit propositions (e.g., "Our Father which art in Heaven" carries implicit propositions about the brotherhood of man) that belong to the belief-dependent category.

The relationship between belief and validity is not linear and total belief does not guarantee total validity. Validity typically reaches a maximum beyond which further increases in belief produce frustrating experiences, leading some to doubt.

### Gödel's Incompleteness and the Limits of Communication Theory

Gödel proved that no system of statements can be both self-contained (explaining its own axioms) and non-self-contradictory — contradictions of the Russellian type must creep in. This means psychology and human communication *can never hope to build a self-contained and coherent system which will not be self-contradictory*.

In practice: when dealing simultaneously with objective communication and metacommunication, contradictions will arise within the field of inquiry itself. We must expect to find in play, art, religion, epistemology, and psychiatric theory paradoxes of the "I am lying" type.

R&B conclude: "we expect our own position, like all others, to be, in the end, either incomplete or self-contradictory."

## Conclusion: Individual, Group, and Culture — A Review

### The Four Levels of Communication Networks

The conclusion systematizes everything into a four-level schema (represented visually as sectors of a cone in Figure 4, and as Table D). The superhuman observer looking from outside human communication systems sees:

**At each level, three functions operate**: reception, evaluation/coordination, and transmission. But the structural properties differ radically:

### Level I — Intrapersonal Network

**Structural properties**:
- The self-observer is *always totally participant* — no separation between observer and observed.
- Both origin and destination of messages are within one organism — error correction is therefore difficult if not impossible.
- The system of codification used *can never be examined* from within.

**Three functional groups**:
1. **Reception**: Proprioception (internal, chemical/mechanical — feelings/sensations) and exteroception (surface-located, wave phenomena — information about self-environment relations). These are complementary but never simultaneously complete: pain may preclude external perceptiveness; violent external events may preclude awareness of pain.
2. **Transmission**: Propriotransmission (efferent nervous impulses to smooth muscles, humoral pathways for regulation) and exterotransmission (striped muscles for action upon outside world and communication).
3. **Central junctions**: Coordination, interpretation, storage of information. Proprioceptive and exteroceptive information is complementary — complete information requires combining both, but the organism specializes at any moment in one or the other mode.

### Level II — Interpersonal Network

**Structural properties**:
- Potentialities for receiving, transmitting, and evaluating are *equally divided* — the system consists of *potentially equivalent parts*. The directional flow characteristic of neural networks is therefore absent.
- Both origin and destination of messages are known to senders and recipients — *correction of information is possible*.
- The observer of others must be *partially participating, partially observing*. When observing, a bigger Gestalt is considered; when participating, the Gestalt narrows. The two types of information complement each other but *complementation is never complete*. The individual can never perceive himself perfectly in relation to others — there is always discrepancy between proprioceptive self-view and exteroceptive/social self-view.

### Level III — Group Network

**Structural properties**:
- Potentialities for receiving and transmitting are *unequally divided* among persons. This specialization re-establishes directional flow of messages. It unites individuals into a larger unit carrying out reception, transmission, and coordination.
- Typically, only the source OR the destination of messages is known — individuals may act as origin/destination or as channels merely relaying messages. *Correction of messages is therefore delayed* and frequently possible only by short-cutting established pathways.

**Two message types**:
1. "One to many": One-way flow center→periphery. Reply delayed. The "one" transmits, the "many" receive.
2. "Many to one": One-way flow periphery→center. Progressive abstraction necessary because of receiver's limited capacity. The "one" receives, the "many" transmit.

**Key property**: Completeness of information obtained by any individual *decreases with every increase in complexity and differentiation*. Specialized functions (observer, transmitter, coordinator) imply impoverished perception. Where two groups are in contact, each group's picture of itself and the other is inflexible, stereotyped, and projective.

### Level IV — Cultural Network

**Structural properties**:
- The individual *cannot recognize the source and destination* of messages and therefore does not recognize the network structure.
- Messages are transmitted from many to many. Sources and destinations are unknown. Potentialities for receiving and transmitting are *unascribed*. *Correction of information is therefore impossible*.
- Participants are *unaware* of being receivers or senders. The message seems an unstated description of their way of living, attributed to no human origin. They transmit by living in accordance with its content, which they regard as "human nature."

**Examples of culturally carried messages**: Messages about language and linguistic systems, ethical premises, theories of man's relation to universe and fellow man. Carried also by printed word, historical/mythological documents and monuments.

### Short Circuits in Larger Networks

Well-established channels are supplemented by short cuts reducing transmission time and intermediary stations. In superpersonal networks, interpersonal links personalize mass communication (e.g., government emissaries). In intrapersonal/interpersonal networks, short circuits convey *alarm signals* that maintain cohesion by warning of threatened dissolution (e.g., anxiety).

### Technical Characteristics of Communication

**Codification across levels**:
- Intrapersonal: Relation between neural/chemical signals and internal/external events. Brain is preponderantly digital, elaborated to handle Gestalten.
- Interpersonal: Symbolization processes of language plus more tenuous nonverbal symbolisms.
- Group: New types of symbolization — organizational patterns leave traces in participating individuals, but since individuals often act as channels not stations, codification requires intactness of organization as a whole. *The group in action possesses the information, not the individual.*
- Cultural: Entirely different from atomistic intrapersonal/interpersonal codification. The organization is beyond the individual's observation. The individual implicitly carries the cultural message in everyday actions, an infinitesimal part of the network.

**Quantitative properties**: Capacity/load of circuits (overload, jamming, underload); threshold conditions for one relay to affect another; time characteristics (refractory period, latency, summation); upkeep, metabolism, and replacement of parts (organizational continuity maintained but constituent parts constantly replaced — assimilation of new parts into organization).

**Stability and adaptability**: Variables defining the steady state + limits of internal change beyond which deviations cannot be corrected = conditions under which irreversible change must occur. Living within limits = the broad purpose of any system.

**Informational state**: All learned characteristics as aggregate of information — previous events as "experience," effects as codified messages/signs. For the observer, data about informational state can only be obtained by observing self-corrective activities. Group informational state is exceedingly difficult to estimate — group experience (e.g., war) produces information found not in individuals alone nor in stored records but in the *changed topology of social communication pathways*.

**Knowledge and action duality**: Every message is both indicative (statement about the past) and imperative (implications for future action). Indicative characteristics are amplified by study of the emanating system; imperative effectiveness is determined by the character of the receiving system.

### Interaction and Self-Correction

Interaction study always requires statements at two or more levels of abstraction: about participating entities and about the *larger entity brought into being by interaction*. Even person-thing interaction involves self-correction. Authoritarian relationships ("treating participants as things") can never be fully described as one-way communication — self-correction requires return of information about action effects.

**Fundamental unpredictability**: At a given instant the individual does not yet have information he will have later when action effects become observable. Predictions about his own later actions must contain guesswork. If rigidly bound by his own guesses, the larger system becomes rigid and incapable of self-correction.

**Interaction at various levels**:
- *Intrapersonal*: Network capacity more or less known; possibilities of rearrangement limited; the organism can somewhat predict its own reactions.
- *Interpersonal*: Capacity still assessable, but topology is undefined — prediction of future events is difficult or impossible.
- *Group*: Network may be large, but specialization makes group behavior more predictable than non-organized aggregates. However, specialization means individuals become *less able to perceive characteristics of the total group from within*.
- *Cultural*: Temporal and spatial limits of network are not perceptible to participants, who cannot perceive topology. Predictability is minimal for participants and exceedingly difficult for the scientific observer.

**At all levels, degree of self-correction is a function of the entity's ability to predict.**

**Information liberates action, action liberates information**: Goal-directed behavior is corrected by feedback, but action also liberates codified information unavailable until action is in progress. This practice-learning relation obtains at all levels.

**Destructive interaction** may result from: (1) incomplete information about self, others, or the system; (2) discrepancies in evaluation — self-maximizing tendency may destroy a larger system instrumental and necessary to the self's existence. In special cases, self-destruction of a smaller entity is instrumental to survival of the larger system. Purpose can only be discussed after delineating the system whose maintenance the action contributes to.

## Key Concepts for sigma-TAP Integration

1. **Four-level architecture as scaffold for L-matrix**: Level I = L11 (self-internal), Level II = L12/L21 (transactional, bidirectional, correctable), Level III = group (new structural properties — directional flow, delayed correction, impoverished perception), Level IV = L22 (cultural — sources unknown, correction impossible, messages carried as premises of living).

2. **Error correction as the fundamental communicative act**: Information about codification systems can only be obtained through observation of self-corrective activities. No learning occurs without error. This is directly relevant to sigma-TAP's trust/adjustment mechanisms.

3. **Deutero-learning**: The bridge between simple learning and character structure. Agents don't just learn specific behaviors — they learn *orientations* to classes of contexts. The instrumental subject creates an instrumental world; the fatalist creates a fatalistic one. Propositions whose validity depends on belief = self-reinforcing praxitive orientations.

4. **Complementarity that is never complete**: At every level, the individual must specialize in one mode of experience and therefore fail to collect information from the other. Complete information is structurally impossible, not merely technically limited.

5. **Group information resides in topology**: At Level III, "the group in action possesses the information, not the individual." Information from group experience lives in the changed topology of communication pathways. Directly relevant to sigma-TAP's topology/family group tracking.

6. **Level IV correction impossibility**: Cultural messages cannot be corrected because sources and destinations are unknown. They are carried as premises of living, attributed to "human nature." This explains why L22 must operate with fundamentally different dynamics than L12/L21.

7. **Metacommunication and Gödelian limits**: Any attempt to build a coherent system at multiple levels of abstraction must end in paradox and contradiction. The system studying itself cannot fully explain its own axioms without contradiction. This is not a defect but a structural feature of communication.

8. **Mutual awareness of perception** as threshold for group formation: The decisive step from mutual responsiveness to genuine group is each participant knowing that the other perceives them. Operational criteria include acknowledgment signals, requests for repetition, deliberate falsehood.

9. **Dominance-dependence ambiguity**: Identical event sequences can be interpreted as different relationship types depending on deutero-learned premises. Participants need not agree on the interpretation. Freedom of interpretation is both constitutive of human relationship and limited by prior character structure.

10. **Every message is both indicative and imperative**: Statements about the past necessarily imply action in the future. The informational and the performative are inseparable at every level.
