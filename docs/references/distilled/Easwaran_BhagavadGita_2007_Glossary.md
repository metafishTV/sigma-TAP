## Core Argument

A glossary does not argue — it maps a lexical field. But Easwaran's glossary of Sanskrit terms reveals the *compositional architecture* of the Bhagavad Gita's conceptual system through a method no chapter-by-chapter reading can reproduce: etymological decomposition. Terms are broken into their Sanskrit roots — ahamkara = aham ("I") + kara ("maker"), ahimsa = a ("not") + himsa ("violence"), avatara = ava ("down") + tri ("to cross") — exposing how the Gita's concepts are literally *constructed* from simpler semantic primitives [pp. 1–12]. This is not mere lexicography: it reveals that the Gita's philosophical vocabulary is itself a system of compositional generation, where complex concepts emerge from the recombination of a finite set of roots — a structure that parallels combinatorial innovation in adjacent-possible dynamics.

The glossary also reveals three taxonomic layers operating simultaneously in the Gita's vocabulary. First, a *faculty psychology*: buddhi (discrimination/intelligence), manas (sensory registration), and ahamkara (self-will/I-making) constitute a tripartite cognitive architecture where each faculty performs distinct structural work [pp. 3, 7, 1]. Second, a *temporal cosmology*: kalpa (4,320 million years = one Day of Brahma), yuga (four-age deterioration cycle from Krita perfection to Kali collapse), and samsara (the round of birth-death-rebirth) establish nested temporal scales from cosmic to individual [pp. 5, 12, 10]. Third, a *processual vocabulary*: yajna (sacrifice/offering sustaining cosmic order), tapas (self-control generating spiritual power), lila (divine play disguising the One as the many), and sadhana (disciplined practice toward Self-realization) describe how agents act within and upon the system [pp. 12, 11, 7, 9]. Each layer addresses a different dimension — cognitive, temporal, practical — but all three converge on the same structural insight: reality is not static substance but dynamic process requiring active participation.

| ahamkara | [aham "I"; kara "maker"] Self-will, separateness; the faculty that constitutes the sense of individual selfhood | The "I-maker" — etymologically reveals agent individuation as an *active construction*, not a given. | ahimsa | [a "not"; himsa "violence"] Nonviolence, doing no injury, wishing no harm | Structural constraint on action: not merely abstaining from violence but actively cultivating harmlessness. | avidya | [a "not"; vidya "wisdom"] Ignorance, lack of wisdom, want of knowledge | The epistemological counterpart to maya: not mere absence of information but active mis-knowing — taking the unreal for real, the transient for eternal. Structurally opposed to prajna and jnana | p. 2 |
| buddhi | Understanding, intelligence; the faculty of discrimination; correct view, idea, purpose | The decision faculty — distinct from manas (sensory input) and ahamkara (self-construction). Buddhi discriminates, evaluates, and directs. In the Gita's cognitive architecture, buddhi reads the field and chooses action | p. 3 |
| dvandva | In Sanskrit grammar, a compound combining two or more words as a pair or group | Structural principle: the Gita's philosophical method works through complementary pairs (prakriti/purusha, kshetra/kshetrajna, sat/asat). The grammatical category reveals an ontological commitment — reality is structured through complementary pairs, not isolated substances | p. 4 |
| jiva | Living being; the finite individual soul identified with separate existence, as opposed to Atman, the eternal Self | The finite agent — jiva is Atman *under the illusion of separateness*. Not a different entity from Atman but the same reality experienced through ahamkara. The jiva/Atman distinction maps the relationship between agent-as-individual and agent-as-whole | p. 5 |
| kalpa | A period in cosmic time equaling one Day of Brahma or 1,000 "great yugas" — 4,320 million years | Macro-temporal scale: creation/dissolution is cyclical, not linear. Each Day of Brahma contains the entire drama of universal manifestation and withdrawal. Establishes that the Gita's framework spans cosmological time | p. 5 |
| kundalini | "The serpent power," spiritual or evolutionary energy described as coiled at the base of the spine, aroused through meditation and yoga | Emergent capacity: latent potential requiring structured practice (sadhana) to activate. Kundalini rises through progressively higher centers of consciousness — a sequential, irreversible transformation with distinct qualitative stages | p. 6 |
| lila | Game; the divine play of the Lord disguising himself as the many beings of this world | Generative process: the universe as *play* rather than mechanism or accident. Reframes creation not as engineering (purposive construction) or chance (random assembly) but as performative self-expression — the One manifesting as the many for no instrumental reason | p. 7 |
| manas | The mind; specifically, the faculty which registers and stores sensory impressions | The input-processing faculty — distinct from buddhi (discrimination) and ahamkara (self-construction). Manas receives and records; it does not evaluate or decide. In the tripartite architecture: manas → buddhi → action, with ahamkara generating the sense of separate agency throughout | p. 7 |
| moksha | Liberation, salvation, illumination | Terminal state — release from samsara. Moksha is not escape FROM the world but transformation of the agent's relationship TO the world. The agent achieves moksha while still embodied (jivanmukti) — liberation is structural, not spatial | p. 7 |
| prajna | [from jna "to know"] A transcendental mode of knowing developed in deep meditation | Meta-cognitive capacity beyond ordinary knowledge (vidya) and discrimination (buddhi). Prajna is knowing without subject-object separation — the knower and known merge. The epistemological correlate of nirvikalpa samadhi | p. 8 |
| samsara | The world of flux; the round of birth, decay, death and rebirth | Cyclical default dynamics: the system's baseline mode is repetition, not progress. Without moksha-directed intervention, agents cycle through manifestation-dissolution indefinitely. Samsara is not a place but a *mode of operation* — the system's default loop | p. 10 |
| tapas | Austerity, control of the senses; the spiritual power acquired through self-control | Constraint-generated capacity: tapas literally *produces* power through restriction. Self-control is not merely ascetic denial but a generative discipline — constraining certain channels concentrates and amplifies energy in others. Etymology links heat (tap) to transformation | p. 11 |
| yajna | Offering, sacrifice, worship | The constraint closure loop: yajna sustains the conditions for further yajna (sacrifice → rain → food → beings → sacrifice). The three-term definition traces a progression from transactional exchange (offering) to self-transcending contribution (sacrifice) to identity-constituting dedication (worship) | p. 12 |
| yuga | An age or eon; four yugas representing steady deterioration from Krita (perfection) through Treta, Dvapara, to Kali (lowest point). 1,000 yuga-cycles = one kalpa | Phase-transition structure: the four yugas model qualitative regime change — not gradual decline but distinct phases with different dynamics. Krishna's incarnation marks the Dvapara–Kali transition. The system at its lowest point, awaiting reset | p. 12 |

## Theoretical & Methodological Implications

**Method**: The glossary employs etymological decomposition as its primary analytical method. Terms are not merely defined but *disassembled into roots*: ahimsa = a + himsa, avatara = ava + tri, ahamkara = aham + kara [pp. 1–2]. This reveals the compositional logic of Sanskrit philosophical vocabulary — complex concepts are built from recombined primitives, not coined ex nihilo. The method implies that understanding a concept requires understanding its *construction*, not just its referent. Etymological decomposition thus functions as a form of structural analysis: each term's meaning is determined by its internal architecture as much as by contextual usage.

**Cognitive architecture**: The glossary reveals a systematic faculty psychology embedded in the Gita's vocabulary. Three terms — manas, buddhi, ahamkara — constitute a tripartite architecture: manas registers sensory data, buddhi discriminates and evaluates, ahamkara generates the sense of separate selfhood that frames all experience [pp. 7, 3, 1]. This is not a casual distinction but a formal model: input (manas) → processing (buddhi) → self-referencing (ahamkara). The addition of prajna [p. 8] as a meta-cognitive mode transcending this architecture (knowing without subject-object division) establishes a four-level framework: sensing → discriminating → self-constructing → transcending. Liberation consists in the fourth mode superseding the third — prajna dissolving ahamkara.

**Temporal cosmology**: The treatment of kalpa, yuga, and samsara [pp. 5, 12, 10] reveals a nested temporal architecture: individual cycles (samsara) within age-phases (yuga) within cosmic days (kalpa). Each level has distinct dynamics — samsara is repetitive, yuga is degradative, kalpa is expansive-contractive. This multi-scale framework means a single event can simultaneously be a repetition (samsara), a phase of decline (yuga), and a moment in cosmic unfolding (kalpa). No single temporal narrative is adequate — reality operates at multiple timescales simultaneously.
